## III. THE TRIGGER DATE — AND THE LAW THE SCHOOL FAILED TO FOLLOW

### A. The mandatory intervention under the Wareham High School Handbook (p. 44)

The handbook provides, verbatim:

> "Upon reaching the fifth unexcused absence in a school year, parents/guardians will be notified. Students will be required to develop an action plan for student's attendance with the Principal or designee, Guidance Counselor and their parents/guardians. The Principal or designee, or Guidance Counselor **shall** make a reasonable effort to meet with the parent or guardian of a student who has 5 or more unexcused absences."

The word "shall" is legally mandatory. Three actions were required on October 27, 2025, the date Angelina reached her fifth unexcused absence: (1) parent notification, (2) development of an action plan, and (3) a reasonable effort by the school to meet with the parent. **None of these actions was taken — not on October 27, 2025, and not in the eight months that followed.**

### B. MGL c. 71, § 37H — the handbook and due-process mandate

MGL c. 71, § 37H is the statute that empowers and obligates the Superintendent to publish the district's student-conduct policies and that defines the procedural backbone of every student-discipline action in a public high school. It provides, in pertinent part:

> "The superintendent of every school district shall publish the district's policies pertaining to the conduct of teachers and students. ... Each school district's policies pertaining to the conduct of students shall include the following: **disciplinary proceedings, including procedures assuring due process**; standards and procedures for suspension and expulsion of students; ... Copies of these policies shall be provided to any person upon request and without cost by the principal of every school within the district."

> "In each school building containing the grades nine to twelve, inclusive, the principal, in consultation with the school council, shall prepare and distribute to each student a **student handbook setting forth the rules pertaining to the conduct of students**. ... The school council shall review the student handbook each spring to consider changes in disciplinary policy to take effect in September of the following school year, but may consider policy changes at any time. The annual review shall cover all areas of student conduct, including but not limited to those outlined in this section."

§ 37H does three things that matter here:

1. **It makes the student handbook itself a statutory instrument.** It is not a courtesy document; it is the "rules pertaining to the conduct of students" that the principal is statutorily required to "prepare and distribute." When a school imposes a credit-withholding penalty under its handbook, it is exercising statutory authority delegated through § 37H — and the school is bound to follow the handbook it published, on pain of acting outside that authority.

2. **It makes "procedures assuring due process" a mandatory content of every student-discipline policy.** A credit-withholding designation that affects a student's grade point average, transcript, and progression to the next grade is, for purposes of § 37H, a disciplinary measure — it removes a benefit the student has otherwise earned by her academic performance. The procedural due process the statute requires includes notice of the charge, an opportunity to respond, and a meaningful review. The school provided none of these to Angelina before imposing NC/CP.

3. **It requires the handbook to be reviewed annually by the school council "to consider changes in disciplinary policy to take effect in September of the following school year ... [covering] all areas of student conduct."** The handbook under which Angelina was penalized must, by § 37H, have been the subject of a documented annual review. If the handbook's text contains the internal contradictions identified in Section V below, the school council's review failed in its statutory duty, and the resulting handbook is a defective § 37H instrument. A penalty imposed under a defective § 37H instrument cannot stand.

### C. MGL c. 71, § 37H½ — the procedural floor for any student-facing removal

§ 37H½ is the Commonwealth's most explicit articulation of the procedural rights that attach when a public school takes an action that removes a student from her established educational placement. The statute governs felony-related suspensions and expulsions, but its procedural architecture — written notice of the charges, written notice of the right to appeal, the five-day appeal window, the three-day hearing, the right to counsel, the right to present evidence, the superintendent's authority to overturn the principal, and a five-day written decision — is the standard the Legislature has set for student-facing removals. It is the floor below which no school action against a student may fall.

§ 37H½ provides, in pertinent part:

> "Upon the issuance of a criminal complaint charging a student with a felony or upon the issuance of a felony delinquency complaint against a student, the principal or headmaster of a school in which the student is enrolled may suspend such student ... if said principal or headmaster determines that the student's continued presence in school would have a substantial detrimental effect on the general welfare of the school. **The student shall receive written notification of the charges and the reasons for such suspension prior to such suspension taking effect. The student shall also receive written notification of his right to appeal and the process for appealing such suspension**; provided, however, that such suspension shall remain in effect prior to any appeal hearing conducted by the superintendent."

> "The student shall have the right to appeal the suspension to the superintendent. **The student shall notify the superintendent in writing of his request for an appeal no later than five calendar days following the effective date of the suspension. The superintendent shall hold a hearing with the student and the student's parent or guardian within three calendar days of the student's request for an appeal.** At the hearing, **the student shall have the right to present oral and written testimony on his behalf, and shall have the right to counsel.** The superintendent shall have the authority to overturn or alter the decision of the principal or headmaster, including recommending an alternate educational program for the student. **The superintendent shall render a decision on the appeal within five calendar days of the hearing.** Such decision shall be the final decision of the city, town or regional school district with regard to the suspension."

By its terms, § 37H½ is the procedural template the General Court has prescribed for any student-facing removal. Its core protections are the procedural backbone the Due Process Clause requires in our schools. *See Goss v. Lopez*, 419 U.S. 565 (1975). Wareham High School provided Angelina with **none** of these protections before imposing NC/CP:

| § 37H½ procedural right | What the school did |
|---|---|
| Written notification of the charges and reasons **prior to** the action taking effect | None. The first written communication arrived on or about June 23, 2026 — the same day the penalty was imposed. |
| Written notification of the right to appeal and the process for appeal | None. The family received no written notice of any appeal process. |
| Five-day window to request an appeal | Not provided. No notice of any window was given. |
| Hearing within three calendar days of the appeal request | Not held. No hearing has been offered or scheduled. |
| Right to present oral and written testimony | Not afforded. |
| Right to counsel at the hearing | Not afforded. |
| Superintendent's written decision within five calendar days of the hearing | Not rendered. The Superintendent has not yet issued a § 37H½-style decision. |

A credit-withholding designation that affects a student's transcript and progression to tenth grade is, in substance if not in name, a removal from her established educational placement. The procedural floor in § 37H½ is the standard against which any such action is measured. The school did not meet that standard. It did not come close. **A penalty imposed without the procedural protections of § 37H½ is, by the General Court's own design in § 37H½, procedurally void.**

### D. The school's duties under MGL c. 76, § 1B

Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 76 — the chapter governing school attendance — establishes duties that are not optional and that this appeal specifically invokes.

**MGL c. 76, § 1B** provides:

> "Each school committee shall have a policy of notifying the parent or guardian of a student who has at least 5 days in which the student has missed 2 or more periods unexcused in a school year or who has missed 5 or more school days unexcused in a school year."

> "The notification policy shall require that the school principal or headmaster, or a designee, make a reasonable effort to meet with the parent or guardian of a student who has 5 or more unexcused absences to develop action steps for student attendance. **The action steps shall be developed jointly and agreed upon by the school principal or headmaster, or a designee, the student, and the parent or guardian.** The policy shall require the principal or headmaster to provide meaningful, ongoing two-way communication between the school, the student, and the parent or guardian."

§ 1B is dispositive here. It required Wareham Public Schools to have a policy with these minimum components:

1. **Notification of the parent** at five unexcused periods (in five days) or five unexcused full days;
2. **A reasonable-effort meeting** between the principal (or designee) and the parent to develop action steps;
3. **Joint development and agreement** on the action steps by the school, the student, and the parent;
4. **Meaningful, ongoing two-way communication** between the school, the student, and the parent.

The school did not establish or follow any such procedure as to Angelina. There was no notification. There was no meeting. There was no action step. There was no joint agreement. There was no two-way communication. For eight months, the school did nothing. Then, in the final week of the school year, it imposed a penalty for the very condition it had a statutory duty to address through engagement rather than punishment.

**§ 1B is not aspirational.** It uses the word "shall" throughout. A school committee that fails to "have" such a policy, and a school that fails to follow it, acts outside the statute. The school cannot satisfy § 1B by skipping every component and then invoking its own absence policy to penalize the result. To permit that reading would render § 1B a nullity — and Massachusetts courts do not read mandatory statutory procedural protections as surplusage.

### E. MGL c. 76, § 2 (parental liability threshold)

§ 2 establishes criminal liability for a parent who "fails to cause [a] child to attend school as required" for **seven or more unexcused full school days in a six-month period**. The statute thus draws a clear line: a parent is exposed to liability at **seven unexcused days in six months**. Angelina's numbers are Semester 1: 7 unexcused (5 Q1 + 2 Q2) — exactly at the threshold; Semester 2: 4 unexcused. She does not clearly and consistently exceed § 2's threshold. More importantly, the school never raised § 2 against me — because it cannot — and any school action premised on a parental-liability rationale is unsupported by the statute.

### F. The 14th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution

The 14th Amendment prohibits any state from depriving "any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." A credit designation that appears on a student's transcript is a recognized property interest in educational benefits. *See Goss v. Lopez*, 419 U.S. 565 (1975). The minimum process due includes notice of the charge and an opportunity to be heard — neither of which the school provided before imposing NC/CP on Angelina. The procedural floor in § 37H½ is the Legislature's implementation of the federal floor in *Goss*. The school fell below both.

### G. MGL c. 119, § 21 (habitual truancy)

Massachusetts defines a "habitual truant" as a student who willfully fails to attend school for more than 8 school days in a quarter. Angelina's highest quarterly unexcused total is five (Q1). She has never reached the statutory threshold in any quarter. **The school has no legal basis to process her as a habitual truant, and no statutory authority to file a Child Requiring Assistance (CRA) petition under MGL c. 119, § 39E.**

### H. DESE Chronic Absenteeism (10% rule)

The Massachusetts Department of Elementary and Secondary Education defines chronic absenteeism as missing 10% or more of all school days. With 180 school days, the threshold is 18 days. Angelina has 16 (8.9%). **She is below the state definition of chronic absenteeism, and the school cannot classify her as chronically absent under DESE standards.**

### I. 603 CMR 23.00 (student records)

Massachusetts regulations grant parents the right to challenge inaccurate, misleading, or procedurally invalid information in a student's record (603 CMR 23.07, 23.09). The NC/CP designation on Angelina's record is based on attendance data accumulated without the mandatory intervention procedures the school was required to follow. Any record reflecting a penalty imposed without due process is, by definition, an inaccurate record. **The parent's right to challenge this record is invoked by separate notice and is independent of this appeal.** The 603 CMR 23.00 timeline is statutory and continues through summer. The Principal has until July 1, 2026 to respond. The Superintendent has until July 15. A School Committee hearing must occur within 30 days. Summer break does not pause any of these deadlines.

## V. THE HANDBOOK'S INTERNAL CONTRADICTIONS

The handbook's text, on its own terms, cannot support the penalty as applied to Angelina:

1. **The excused/unexcused distinction is selectively invoked.** Page 44 keys the intervention trigger to "unexcused" absences. Page 45 keys the NC designation to "9 total days absent" without that distinction. The school cannot simultaneously claim the distinction does not exist (p. 43) and rely on it (p. 44) and ignore it (p. 45).
2. **The intervention safety net is broken by design.** A student can hit NC at 9 total days absent (p. 45) without the 5-unexcused intervention (p. 44) ever firing, because excused absences count toward NC but do not count toward the intervention trigger. The handbook's "intervention" is not an actual safety net for any student whose absences are mixed excused and unexcused.
3. **Tardies are an unwritten standard.** PowerSchool records 27 tardies. The handbook's CP/NC framework (pp. 42–45) contains no mention of tardies. Imposing credit consequences for tardies under an unwritten standard is procedurally invalid.
4. **The handbook was not properly reviewed under § 37H.** § 37H requires the school council to "review the student handbook each spring to consider changes in disciplinary policy to take effect in September of the following school year." If the contradictions above were not identified, corrected, and documented in a spring review, the § 37H review failed and the handbook is a defective § 37H instrument.

Ambiguity in a school-imposed rule is construed against the school. A defective § 37H instrument cannot support a penalty. The handbook cannot be read to penalize what it does not prohibit.
