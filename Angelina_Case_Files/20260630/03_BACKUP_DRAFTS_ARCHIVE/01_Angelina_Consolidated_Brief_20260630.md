## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Wareham High School administration is requiring Angelina Crooker to attend summer school and has placed Credit Pending (CP) and No Credit (NC) designations on her academic record for the 2025-2026 school year. The penalty rests on sixteen absences — eleven unexcused, five excused — recorded in the school's own PowerSchool system. The school provided no prior notification of these absences to the parent in any form, despite the school handbook's mandatory intervention requirements and Massachusetts statutory protections. The penalty is void on multiple independent grounds: failure to follow the school's own mandatory intervention procedure, failure to provide procedural due process, application of an internally contradictory attendance policy, and reliance on absence figures below every Massachusetts and DESE threshold for the consequences imposed.

## VII. THE LEGAL VIOLATIONS

The school has violated the following:

### A. State Law

1. **M.G.L. c. 76, § 1B** — Failed to establish and uniformly apply a policy of (a) reasonable-effort notification for absences, (b) meeting-and-plan development for repeated absence, and (c) meeting-and-plan development for habitual absence or truancy.
2. **M.G.L. c. 119, § 21** — Failed to satisfy the pre-filing intervention requirements before any truancy-related proceeding. (No CRA petition has been filed; this protective invocation preserves the issue.)
3. **M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H** — Failed to provide the procedural protections of due process that apply to the imposition of educational deprivations. Specifically, the school did not provide written notice of the proposed action, an opportunity to be heard, or a written decision on appeal.
4. **M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H1/2** — Failed to provide the procedural template applicable to serious educational deprivations: written notice, opportunity for a hearing before the Superintendent within a defined timeline, right to counsel, right to present evidence, and right to a written decision. These protections apply to attendance-driven credit penalties, which are recognized property interests under *Goss v. Lopez*, 419 U.S. 565 (1975).

### B. Federal Law

5. **14th Amendment, U.S. Constitution** — Procedural due process denied. A credit designation on a transcript is a recognized property interest. Notice and an opportunity to be heard are minimum requirements. Neither was provided.
6. **FERPA, 20 U.S.C. § 1232g and 34 CFR Part 99** — Right to inspect, seek amendment, hearing on refusal, and statement of disagreement all engaged. See FERPA Complaint for full specification.
7. **Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, 29 U.S.C. § 794** — Engaged by the school's documented awareness of bullying and the failure to engage the interactive process that would have identified needed accommodations.

### C. State Regulations

8. **603 CMR 23.07 and 23.09** — Right to challenge inaccurate or misleading information in the student record invoked. Timeline: Principal (one week — July 1, 2026), Superintendent (two weeks — July 15, 2026), School Committee hearing (within 30 days). Calendar-day timeline. Summer break does not pause, toll, or extend any deadline.

### D. DESE Policy

9. **DESE Chronic Absenteeism Guidance** — 18 days is the threshold. Angelina has 16 (8.9%). The school cannot classify her as chronically absent.

## VIII. THE STRONGEST LEGAL ARGUMENTS

### Argument 1: The Handbook Mandates Intervention Before Penalty; the School Skipped Every Step

The handbook's plain text makes the intervention sequence mandatory ("shall"). The school performed none of the three required actions between October 27, 2025 and June 23, 2026. The penalty imposed at the endpoint is therefore void. This argument requires no statutory citation and stands on the school's own published policy.

### Argument 2: The Penalty Exists Only Because the School Suppressed the Intervention

The school cannot use its own procedural failures as a basis for penalty. Had it intervened at five unexcused absences on October 27, 2025, the parent would have been engaged, an action plan would have been in place, and Quarter 2-4 attendance would likely have been different. The same system that guaranteed Angelina intervention is the system now penalizing her for the consequences of that intervention never occurring.

### Argument 3: The Handbook Is Internally Contradictory and Cannot Be Enforced As Applied

Ambiguity is construed against the drafter. A policy that simultaneously states "no differentiation between excused and unexcused absences" (p. 43) and applies every trigger and credit bar only to unexcused absences (pp. 44-45) is not a policy that can produce an accurate record. The NC/CP designation rests on the application of contradictory rules.

### Argument 4: No Massachusetts or DESE Threshold for the Consequences Imposed Is Met

Angelina is not a habitual truant (Q1=5 < 8). She is not chronically absent (16 < 18). The parent is not exposed to § 2 liability (S1=7 at threshold, S2=4). She is not barred from credit retrieval (11 < 20). The only thresholds she meets are the ones created by the school's own failure.

### Argument 5: Procedural Due Process Under the 14th Amendment Is Engaged

A credit designation on a transcript is a recognized property interest under *Goss v. Lopez*. The school provided no notice of the proposed penalty, no opportunity to be heard, and no written decision. The procedural void is constitutional in dimension.

## Disability-Related Framework, Bullying Response, and IEP/504 Obligations

**Note to the reader:** The eighteen violations enumerated below were identified after the initial case brief and correspondence were prepared. They address the school's failure to engage the disability-related framework — specifically, Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, Title II of the ADA, the IDEA child-find obligation, and Massachusetts antibullying law (M.G.L. c. 71, § 37O). The parent did not have this information at the time of the initial June 23-24, 2026 correspondence. These violations are independent of the procedural and policy violations identified in the main brief. Each is independently sufficient to render the penalty void.

## THE ADDITIONAL EIGHTEEN VIOLATIONS

### CATEGORY I — PROCEDURAL DUE PROCESS VIOLATIONS (1–10)

**Violation 1 — Failure to provide written notice of the charge (M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H1/2).**
M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H1/2 requires written notice of any charge that may result in a serious sanction. The school did not provide written notice that Angelina's attendance record would be treated as a charge subject to a credit-withholding penalty. The first communication of the penalty was the imposition of the penalty itself.

**Violation 2 — Failure to provide the right to a hearing before the Superintendent (M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H1/2).**
Section 37H1/2 requires a hearing before the Superintendent (or designee) on the proposed sanction. No hearing was offered. The penalty was imposed unilaterally by the building administration.

**Violation 3 — Failure to provide the right to counsel or representative (M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H1/2).**
Section 37H1/2 guarantees the right to be represented by counsel or a representative at the hearing. No such right was communicated, and no hearing at which the right could be exercised was convened.

**Violation 4 — Failure to provide the right to present evidence and witnesses (M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H1/2).**
Section 37H1/2 guarantees the right to present evidence and to question witnesses. The school did not identify the evidence on which the penalty rested, did not identify the witnesses, and did not provide a forum in which evidence could be presented.

**Violation 5 — Failure to provide a written decision (M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H1/2).**
Section 37H1/2 requires a written decision after hearing. The school provided no written decision explaining the basis of the penalty, the evidence relied on, or the reasoning applied.

**Violation 6 — Failure to provide a meaningful right of appeal (M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H1/2 and § 37H).**
Section 37H1/2 and § 37H both require an appeal mechanism to the Superintendent and the School Committee. The school did not provide an appeal mechanism. The penalty was imposed in the final week of the school year with no time afforded for the statutory appeal process to run.

**Violation 7 — Failure to engage the mandatory Page 44 intervention procedure (M.G.L. c. 76, § 1B).**
M.G.L. c. 76, § 1B requires each school committee to establish a policy of reasonable-effort notification and meeting-and-plan development, and to apply that policy uniformly. The Wareham High School Handbook at Page 44 makes the intervention at five unexcused absences mandatory ("shall"). The school performed none of the three required actions between October 27, 2025 and June 23, 2026.

**Violation 8 — Failure to provide notice and an opportunity to be heard (14th Amendment; *Goss v. Lopez*, 419 U.S. 565 (1975)).**
The 14th Amendment requires the State to provide notice and an opportunity to be heard before depriving a student of a property interest in education. *Goss v. Lopez* establishes that a transcript-level credit designation is such a deprivation. The school provided neither notice nor a hearing.

**Violation 9 — Failure to engage the records-challenge procedure on the statutory timeline (603 CMR 23.07).**
The parent invoked the right to challenge inaccurate, misleading, or procedurally invalid information in the student record. The Principal has until July 1, 2026 to respond. The Principal has not engaged the timeline.

**Violation 10 — Failure to engage the Superintendent appeal procedure on the statutory timeline (603 CMR 23.08/23.09).**
The parent's appeal to the Superintendent triggers a July 15, 2026 response deadline and a 30-day Fair Hearing window. The Superintendent has not engaged the timeline.

### CATEGORY II — DISABILITY-RELATED VIOLATIONS (11–18)

**Violation 11 — Failure to engage the Section 504 interactive process (34 CFR § 104.36).**
The school had reason to believe Angelina may have needed accommodation, based on (i) the documented pattern of late-day absences, (ii) the school's awareness of bullying incidents, and (iii) the academic-against-attendance divergence. The school did not initiate the interactive process.

**Violation 12 — Failure to provide the Section 504 procedural safeguards notice (34 CFR § 104.36).**
A school district must provide a procedural safeguards notice when a student is receiving Section 504 services or is the subject of a Section 504 referral. The school provided neither.

**Violation 13 — Failure to conduct child-find under Section 504 (34 CFR § 104.35).**
The school had actual knowledge of circumstances triggering a duty to identify and evaluate. The school did not conduct the evaluation.

**Violation 14 — Failure to investigate and respond to bullying under M.G.L. c. 71, § 37O.**
Massachusetts antibullying law requires the school to investigate and respond to reports of bullying. The school has not produced evidence of an investigation or response.

**Violation 15 — Failure to consider disability-related attendance as a basis for reasonable modification (28 CFR § 35.130; Title II ADA).**
The attendance enforcement policy, as applied to a student with disability-related experiences, requires reasonable modification. The school did not engage the interactive process that would have identified the modification.

**Violation 16 — Failure to conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment when pattern indicated need (34 CFR § 104.35; M.G.L. c. 71B).**
The documented pattern of late-day absences and the bullying awareness together indicated the need for a Functional Behavioral Assessment. None was conducted.

**Violation 17 — Failure to convene a 504 Team or special education Team to determine evaluation (M.G.L. c. 71B; 603 CMR 28.00).**
The parent has requested an evaluation. The school has not convened a Team to determine whether evaluation is warranted. The IDEA child-find obligation is independent of any parental request and is itself violated.

**Violation 18 — Disability-based penalty (Section 504; Title II ADA).**
The penalty imposed on Angelina — summer school and NC/CP designations — is disability-based in its effect. The penalty rests on absences driven, in whole or in part, by disability-related experiences of which the school was aware.

## SUMMARY

The additional eighteen violations are distinct procedural and substantive failures, each independently sufficient to void the action complained of. Cure of one does not cure the others.
