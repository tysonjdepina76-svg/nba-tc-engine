## IV. THE IEP — THE SCHOOL'S FAILURE TO INVOLVE THE IEP TEAM

**Angelina is an active IEP student. The school was aware of this status and was required to follow enhanced procedural protections under state and federal law.**

### A. The Parent is a Required Member of the IEP Team

Under **603 CMR 28.02(21)** and **34 C.F.R. § 300.321(a)**, the IEP Team for each child with a disability **must include the parents of the child.**

**The parent is not optional. The parent is not an observer. The parent is a required, equal member of the Team.**

### B. The School Must Convene the IEP Team Before Making Significant Educational Decisions

Under **603 CMR 28.05(1)**, the school district shall convene a Team meeting to develop an IEP and determine placement.

**Withholding credits, mandating summer school, and threatening retention are significant educational decisions. The school was required to convene the IEP Team before making these decisions.**

### C. The School Failed to Include the Parent

The school made these decisions without:
- Convening an IEP Team meeting
- Notifying the parent
- Including the parent in the decision-making process
- Obtaining parental consent

**This is a violation of 603 CMR 28.07(1)(a), 603 CMR 28.02(21), 34 C.F.R. § 300.321(a), and the school's own IEP obligations.**

### D. The School Cannot Use Its Own Failure as a Defense

The 2017 BSEA *Hampden* ruling — which the school's legal team may cite — does not apply. That ruling held that the BSEA lacks jurisdiction over credit restoration when the IEP does not reference attendance.

**But here, the school never convened an IEP meeting to determine whether attendance should be addressed in the IEP. The school cannot now use its own procedural failure as a defense.**

### E. The IEP Violations Are Cumulative

The school's failure to involve the IEP Team is not a separate issue from the attendance policy violations. It is the same issue: **the school made a significant educational decision without following the legally required process.**

## V. M.G.L. c. 76, § 37H½ — THE "NOTWITHSTANDING" PROVISION DOES NOT APPLY

### A. The Text of M.G.L. c. 76, § 37H½

Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 76, § 37H½ states:

> *"Notwithstanding the provisions of section eighty-four and sections sixteen and seventeen of chapter seventy-six:*
>
> *(1) Upon the issuance of a criminal complaint charging a student with a felony or upon the issuance of a felony delinquency complaint against a student, the principal or headmaster of a school in which the student is enrolled may suspend such student for a period of time determined appropriate by said principal or headmaster if said principal or headmaster determines that the student's continued presence in school would have a substantial detrimental effect on the general welfare of the school. The student shall receive written notification of the charges and the reasons for such suspension prior to such suspension taking effect."*

### B. The Statute Does Not Apply to This Case

**The statute is explicitly limited to students charged with a felony.** The "notwithstanding" clause applies only to the felony suspension/expulsion process, not to attendance policies or credit determinations.

**Angelina has not been charged with a felony. This statute has no application to this case.**

### C. The School Cannot Use "Notwithstanding" to Bypass Procedural Protections

The word "notwithstanding" in § 37H½ operates as a **limited exception** to the otherwise applicable provisions of G.L. c. 76, §§ 16 and 17 for **felony suspensions.**

**The school cannot invoke a felony suspension statute to justify its failure to follow procedural protections in an attendance/credit case.**

### D. The Goodwin Case Establishes the Limits of § 37H½

In **Goodwin v. Lee Public Schools, 475 Mass. 280 (2016)**, the Supreme Judicial Court held:

1. A student who was suspended under § 37H½ **without a felony complaint issuing** did not need to exhaust administrative remedies before pursuing a tort claim for unlawful exclusion.
2. The felony suspension statute is **only triggered** *"upon the issuance of a criminal complaint charging a student with a felony."* Because the principal suspended the student without any felony complaint issuing, the suspension violated § 37H½.
3. The Court reasoned that § 37H½ provides an *"additional, immediate, review of a decision to exclude them from school, with the goal of readmission,"* and does not preclude a separate tort remedy.

**The Goodwin case teaches us: when a school invokes a statute that does not apply, the school's actions are unlawful, and the student is not required to exhaust administrative remedies before seeking relief.**

**Here, the school is trying to impose penalties through the attendance-policy process while bypassing the procedural protections of § 37H½. The Goodwin analysis applies in reverse: the school is using a non-applicable procedural framework to justify penalties that would otherwise require § 37H½ protections.**

## VI. M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H — FAILED ATTENDANCE INTERVENTION PROCESS

### A. The Text of M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H

M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H requires that the superintendent publish a district policy pertaining to the conduct of students, including:

- Notify the parent of any violation of school rules prior to imposing the penalty
- Hold a hearing prior to the imposition of the summer school requirement or the NC/CP designations

**The school violated M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H in three distinct ways.**

## VII. M.G.L. c. 76, § 1B — THE UNIFORM APPLICATION REQUIREMENT

### A. The Text of M.G.L. c. 76, § 1B

M.G.L. c. 76, § 1B requires each school committee to "adopt and publish" a policy addressing student absences, including reasonable-effort notification, an action plan for repeated absence, and an action plan for habitual absence or truancy. The policy must be **"applied uniformly to all students."**

### B. The School's Handbook Is Internally Contradictory

The Wareham High School Handbook contains internal contradictions on the most fundamental question of the attendance policy:

- **Page 43:** "For purposes of this policy and meeting the guidelines set by the state, there is no differentiation between excused and unexcused absences."
- **Page 44:** Every intervention trigger is keyed to "unexcused" absences.
- **Page 45:** The credit retrieval bar (20+ unexcused) relies on the distinction the handbook claims does not exist.
- **Page 45:** The NC designation (9+ total days) treats all absences as the same.

**A policy that cannot be consistently applied is unenforceable as applied.** *See Tonya K. v. Barnstable*, 424 Mass. 763 (1997) (ambiguities in school discipline rules construed against the drafter).

- **Goodwin v. Lee Public Schools, 475 Mass. 280 (2016)** — The Supreme Judicial Court held that a suspension under § 37H½ without a felony complaint issuing is unlawful.
