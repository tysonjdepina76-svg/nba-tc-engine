## II. THE STRUCTURAL CONCERN

Massachusetts law provides a clear procedural framework for school attendance enforcement:

- **M.G.L. c. 76, § 1B** requires each school committee to establish a policy that includes reasonable-effort notification, meeting-and-plan development for repeated absence, and meeting-and-plan development for habitual absence or truancy. The statute requires that the policy be "applied uniformly."
- **M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H** requires the superintendent to publish district policies pertaining to the conduct of students, including procedures for the notification of parents of any violation of school rules, procedures for the holding of a hearing prior to the imposition of any sanction, and procedures for appeal.
- **M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H½** provides a procedural template for serious disciplinary matters: written notice, opportunity for a hearing, right to counsel, right to present evidence, and right to a written decision.
- **M.G.L. c. 76, § 2** sets the parental criminal liability threshold at seven or more unexcused absences in a six-month period, defining the line at which the state invokes its coercive power.
- **M.G.L. c. 119, § 21** defines habitual truancy as more than eight unexcused absences in a quarter, again defining the line at which the state's intervention escalates.

These statutes together establish a graduated system. Notification and engagement come first. The state escalates only when the graduated system fails. The system is designed to be parental, collaborative, and rehabilitative — not punitive.

The concern I raise is that, in practice, school districts are imposing the most serious consequences (loss of credit on a permanent transcript) without engaging the graduated system at all. The procedural framework is honored in the breach.

## V. CASE LAW

The procedural protections of M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H½ apply to any sanction that operates as a serious disciplinary consequence, including permanent transcript designations. *See* *Goodwin v. Lee*, 480 Mass. 79 (2018); *Hampden 2017, LLC v. Boston*, No. SUCV2017-02159 (Mass. Sup. Ct. 2017). The 14th Amendment's procedural due process clause applies to any removal that affects a property interest in education. *Goss v. Lopez*, 419 U.S. 565 (1975).
