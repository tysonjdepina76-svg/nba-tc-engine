## I. SUMMARY OF THE DISPUTE

The Wareham High School administration is requiring Angelina Crooker to attend summer school and has placed Credit Pending and No Credit designations on her academic record. The school's position rests on sixteen total absences — eleven unexcused, five excused — recorded in its own PowerSchool system.

The parent contests this penalty because the school failed to follow its own published attendance intervention policy, failed to provide any notification before imposing the penalty, applied an internally contradictory handbook that cannot be consistently enforced, and imposed consequences that Massachusetts General Laws and DESE guidelines do not support.

The school did not notify the parent of Angelina's attendance at any point during the 2025-2026 school year. The first communication occurred on or about June 23, 2026 — the final week — and consisted solely of the penalty itself.

## III. THE TRIGGER DATE AND THE HANDBOOK'S MANDATORY INTERVENTION

On October 27, 2025, Angelina reached her fifth unexcused absence. This date is not disputed. It is recorded in PowerSchool, confirmed in Dean Cinelli's email, and stated in the school's own June 23 communication.

The Wareham High School Handbook, Page 44, states:

> "Upon reaching the fifth unexcused absence in a school year, parents/guardians will be notified. Students will be required to develop an action plan for student's attendance with the Principal or designee, Guidance Counselor and their parents/guardians. The Principal or designee, or Guidance Counselor shall make a reasonable effort to meet with the parent or guardian of a student who has 5 or more unexcused absences."

Three mandatory actions were required:

1. The parent would be notified.
2. An action plan would be developed.
3. The school would make a reasonable effort to meet with the parent.

The word "shall" is mandatory. None of these actions was performed — not on October 27, 2025, and not in the eight months that followed.

## V. INTERNAL CONTRADICTIONS IN THE HANDBOOK

The handbook cannot be consistently applied. It contradicts itself on the most basic question of the attendance policy — whether excused and unexcused absences are treated differently:

| Page | Policy Statement |
|------|-----------------|
| 43 | "For purposes of this policy and meeting the guidelines set by the state, there is no differentiation between excused and unexcused absences." |
| 44 | Every notification and intervention trigger applies only to unexcused absences. |
| 45 | The credit retrieval bar uses "20 or more unexcused absences" — a distinction the handbook elsewhere says does not exist. |
| 45 | NC is imposed at 9 total days absent — counting all absences without distinction. |

Because the handbook cannot state a single, consistent rule for whether excused and unexcused absences are the same or different, it cannot produce a reliable record or a fair result. Where school policies are ambiguous, Massachusetts courts construe them against the drafter. See Tonya K. v. Barnstable School Committee, 424 Mass. 763 (1997).

## VII. LEGAL VIOLATIONS

### A. State Law

1. **MGL c. 76, § 1B** — The school committee must establish and uniformly apply a policy of reasonable-effort notification for absences and meeting-and-plan development for repeated absences. The handbook does so on Page 44. The school did not apply it.

2. **MGL c. 119, § 21** — Before pursuing truancy proceedings, the school must make a reasonable effort to notify and meet. No such effort was made. No CRA petition has been filed. This is raised to preserve the issue.

3. **MGL c. 71, § 37H** — The procedural protections of due process apply to the imposition of educational deprivations. The school did not provide written notice of the proposed action, an opportunity to be heard, or a written decision on appeal.

4. **MGL c. 71, § 37H½** — A serious educational sanction — including a permanent transcript credit designation — requires written notice, a hearing before the Superintendent, the right to counsel, the right to present evidence, and a written decision. None of these were provided. Credit designations are recognized property interests. Goss v. Lopez, 419 U.S. 565 (1975).

### B. Federal Law

5. **14th Amendment, U.S. Constitution** — Procedural due process was denied. A credit designation on a transcript is a protected property interest requiring notice and an opportunity to be heard. Neither was provided.

6. **FERPA, 20 U.S.C. § 1232g and 34 CFR Part 99** — The parent's right to inspect records, seek amendment, obtain a hearing on refusal, and place a statement of disagreement in the record has been invoked. (See separately filed FERPA Complaint.)

### C. State Regulations

7. **603 CMR 23.07 and 23.09** — The parent challenges the accuracy of the student record. Timeline: Principal has until July 1, 2026 to respond. Superintendent has until July 15, 2026. School Committee hearing must be held within 30 days. These deadlines run on calendar days and are not suspended by summer break.

### D. DESE Policy

8. **DESE Chronic Absenteeism Guidance** — The threshold is 18 days (10%). Angelina has 16 (8.9%). She cannot be classified as chronically absent under DESE standards.

## VIII. LEGAL ARGUMENTS

### Argument 1: The Handbook Requires Intervention Before Penalty

The handbook's plain language makes the intervention sequence mandatory. The school did not perform any of the three required actions. The penalty imposed without the required intervention is void.

### Argument 2: The Penalty Results From the School's Own Failure

The school cannot rely on its procedural failures as the basis for a penalty. If the school had intervened on October 27, 2025 as required, the parent would have been notified, an action plan would have been in place, and Angelina's attendance pattern could have been addressed before it reached the NC threshold.

### Argument 3: The Handbook Is Internally Contradictory

A policy that cannot consistently state whether excused and unexcused absences are the same or different cannot be enforced against a particular student. The NC/CP designation rests on the application of contradictory rules.

### Argument 4: Angelina Does Not Meet Any Massachusetts or DESE Threshold for the Penalty

Angelina is not a habitual truant. She is not chronically absent. Her parent is not exposed to criminal liability. She is not barred from credit retrieval. The penalty exceeds what Massachusetts law and DESE policy support for a student with her absence record.

### Argument 5: Procedural Due Process Was Denied

The school provided no notice of the proposed penalty, no opportunity to be heard, and no written decision. A permanent credit designation without notice or a hearing is a violation of the 14th Amendment.

## ADDENDUM — ADDITIONAL DISABILITY-RELATED INFORMATION

This addendum contains information that was not known to the parent at the time the initial demand letters were submitted. The parent has since learned that the school was on notice of circumstances suggesting Angelina may have needed evaluation or accommodation related to a disability. The school was aware of documented bullying, a pattern of absences, and a divergence between her attendance record and her academic performance. The school did not act on this information under Section 504, the IDEA, or Massachusetts law.

The following violations are identified for the record. They are presented separately because they rest on information the school possessed but did not disclose or act upon. The parent raises them now to preserve all available remedies.

### Disability-Related Failures

1. **Failure to initiate the Section 504 interactive process** (34 CFR § 104.36). The school was aware of bullying, attendance patterns, and the attendance-to-academic-performance gap. It did not engage the interactive process.

2. **Failure to provide Section 504 procedural safeguards notice** (34 CFR § 104.36). When a student may need Section 504 services, the district must provide notice. It did not.

3. **Failure to conduct child-find under Section 504** (34 CFR § 104.35). The school had actual knowledge of circumstances triggering a duty to evaluate. It did not evaluate.

4. **Failure to investigate and respond to bullying** (MGL c. 71, § 37O). Massachusetts law requires investigation and response. The school has not produced evidence of either.

5. **Failure to consider reasonable modification for disability-related absences** (28 CFR § 35.130; Title II ADA). The attendance policy, as applied to a student with disability-related experiences, requires reasonable modification. No modification was considered because no process was initiated.

6. **Failure to conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment** (34 CFR § 104.35; MGL c. 71B). The pattern of absences and known bullying indicated the need for assessment. None was conducted.

7. **Failure to convene a Team to determine eligibility for evaluation** (MGL c. 71B; 603 CMR 28.00). The IDEA child-find obligation is independent of any parent request and continues regardless.

8. **Disability-based penalty** (Section 504; Title II). The penalty imposed rests, in whole or in part, on disability-related experiences the school knew about and did not address.

This addendum is submitted for the record and does not alter or limit the primary legal arguments set forth in the main body of this brief.
