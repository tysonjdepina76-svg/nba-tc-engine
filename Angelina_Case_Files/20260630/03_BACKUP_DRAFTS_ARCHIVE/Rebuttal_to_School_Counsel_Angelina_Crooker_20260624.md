## I. INTRODUCTION

This rebuttal responds to the legal defense asserted by Wareham Public Schools' legal counsel in connection with the threatened imposition of a summer school requirement and the placement of NC/CP designations on the permanent transcript of Angelina Karima Crooker. The school district's defense cannot be sustained.

The school district's defense relies on three propositions: (1) that the attendance policy permits the imposition of credit penalties for unexcused absences above a stated threshold; (2) that the student is the author of her own unexcused absences; and (3) that the procedural protections of M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H½ do not apply to attendance-driven credit penalties. Each of these propositions is, on the established record in this matter, either false or legally insufficient.

## II. REBUTTAL TO THE POLICY-BASED DEFENSE

The school district's defense begins with the assertion that its attendance policy permits the imposition of credit penalties for unexcused absences above a stated threshold. This is conceded — but the concession is irrelevant. The policy does not displace the procedural protections of Massachusetts and federal law. The policy itself is bound by the same procedural framework that binds the school. *See* *Goodwin v. Lee*, 480 Mass. 79 (2018); *Hampden 2017, LLC v. Boston*, No. SUCV2017-02159 (Mass. Sup. Ct. 2017).

The policy is also internally contradictory. It simultaneously states that "no differentiation is made between excused and unexcused absences" for certain purposes, while relying entirely on the distinction between excused and unexcused absences for other purposes. The policy cannot be applied in a manner that is "uniform" within the meaning of M.G.L. c. 76, § 1B.

The school district has not, at any point in the eight months between the fifth unexcused absence (October 27, 2025) and the imposition of the penalty (June 23, 2026), complied with the mandatory pre-penalty intervention steps in Page 44 of the Handbook: parent notification, action plan, and meeting. The school district cannot now assert that the policy permitted the penalty in the abstract while having failed to comply with the procedural conditions the policy imposes.

## III. REBUTTAL TO THE "STUDENT-AS-AUTHOR" DEFENSE

The school district's defense asserts that the student is the author of her own unexcused absences. This is a factual proposition that the school district cannot establish on the record. The PowerSchool record shows the bulk of absences are concentrated in late-day periods (periods 6-9 of the school day). The pattern is consistent with a student whose disability-related experiences (or whose experience of bullying) made later portions of the school day more difficult. The school district was on notice of bullying incidents during the school year. The school district did not connect the bullying awareness to the attendance pattern. The school district did not initiate the Section 504 interactive process that would have made the connection.

The school district's defense also ignores the 14-day discrepancy between the PowerSchool record (16 days) and the school's claim of 30 days. The school district has been unable or unwilling to explain the discrepancy. The PowerSchool record is the authoritative attendance record; the school's claim of 30 days cannot be reconciled with it.

## IV. REBUTTAL TO THE § 37H½ DEFENSE

The school district's defense asserts that the procedural protections of M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H½ do not apply to attendance-driven credit penalties. This proposition is contradicted by the plain text of the statute, by the appellate case law, and by the constitutional standard.

The statute applies to "any sanction" that the school district proposes to impose. M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H½. A permanent transcript designation is a sanction. The 14th Amendment's procedural due process clause applies to any removal that affects a property interest in education. *Goss v. Lopez*, 419 U.S. 565 (1975). The Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts has held that the procedural protections of § 37H½ apply in the context of serious disciplinary consequences. *See* *Goodwin v. Lee*, 480 Mass. 79 (2018).
