## PURPOSE

This document is a point-by-point rebuttal to the defenses that the school's legal counsel is anticipated to raise in response to the parent's administrative complaints, the school-level records challenge, the appeal to the Superintendent and School Committee, the DESE PRS complaint, the FERPA complaint, the Section 504 complaint to OCR, and the protective motion filed in Plymouth Juvenile Court. ## I. ANTICIPATED DEFENSE 1: "ANGELINA'S ABSENCES WERE EXCESSIVE"

### School's Likely Position
"Angelina missed 16 days, which is more than the handbook's threshold for the NC designation. The school acted within its authority in imposing NC and requiring summer school."

### Rebuttal

The school's framing of the absences as the controlling fact is the procedural violation. The handbook's NC designation is the penalty phase of a process. The intervention phase — required at the five-unexcused threshold on October 27, 2025 — is the prerequisite. The handbook is structured as a graduated system in which intervention must precede escalation:

| Phase | Page | Required Action | Taken? |
|-------|------|-----------------|--------|
| Notification (2 unexcused) | 44 | Parent notification | NO |
| Notification (5 unexcused) | 44 | Parent notification | NO |
| Action plan (5 unexcused) | 44 | Joint plan with parent | NO |
| Meeting (5 unexcused) | 44 | Reasonable effort by school | NO |
| NC (9 total days) | 45 | Penalty imposed | YES — at the endpoint, after every prerequisite was skipped |

A penalty imposed without its prerequisite procedures is not "within the school's authority." It is contrary to the procedure the school itself established. The authority to impose a consequence under a graduated procedural system is conditional on completion of the prior phases. The condition was not met.

**The 16 absences are the symptom. The procedural failure is the disease. The school cannot defend the disease by reference to the symptom it caused.**

## II. ANTICIPATED DEFENSE 2: "THE EXCUSED/UNEXCUSED DISTINCTION IS INTERNAL TO THE HANDBOOK AND NOT BINDING"

### School's Likely Position
"Pages 43-45 contain internal contradictions, but the school applies the policy in good faith. The handbook's internal inconsistencies are not a basis for invalidating the action."

### Rebuttal

Massachusetts case law directly addresses the situation. *Tonya K. v. Barnstable*, 424 Mass. 763 (1997), holds that ambiguities in school discipline rules are construed against the drafter. *See also*, MGL c. 71, § 37H (the school shall publish the district's policies in writing; written policies must be capable of consistent application). A school policy that cannot be consistently applied is not enforceable as applied. The parent is not required to identify "the right reading" of an internally contradictory policy. The parent is entitled to have the contradiction resolved in her favor.

The four contradictions in the handbook are not marginal. They go to the most fundamental question of the policy — whether excused and unexcused absences are treated the same or differently. The school cannot claim both simultaneously and then impose a penalty under a reading it selected after the fact.

**The school's "good faith" defense is unavailable when the school itself drafted the contradictory text.**

## III. ANTICIPATED DEFENSE 3: "STUDENT IS HABITUALLY TRUANT"

### School's Likely Position
"Angelina's pattern of absences indicates habitual truancy. The school has authority to file a CRA petition if necessary."

### Rebuttal

MGL c. 119, § 21 defines habitual truancy as more than eight unexcused absences in a quarter. Angelina's quarterly totals are 5, 2, 4, and 0. She has never met the statutory threshold in any quarter. She is not a habitual truant under Massachusetts law. The school has no authority to file a CRA petition under MGL c. 119, § 39E.

The Plymouth Juvenile Court has been moved to bar any such filing. Any CRA petition filed in violation of the court's order would expose the school and the district to sanctions.

**The school cannot create jurisdiction for a court process by characterizing facts that do not meet the statutory definition as if they did.**

## IV. ANTICIPATED DEFENSE 4: "ABSENCES ARE JUSTIFIED GROUNDS FOR NC"

### School's Likely Position
"Page 45 of the handbook authorizes NC at 9+ total days. Sixteen days clearly exceeds the threshold. The handbook applies."

### Rebuttal

The Page 45 NC threshold of 9 total days is a single sentence embedded in a policy that contains its own safety net. That safety net is the Page 44 intervention at 5 unexcused days. The school cannot cherry-pick the penalty phase of a graduated policy and ignore the intervention phase. To do so would render the intervention phase surplusage — a result Massachusetts statutory construction does not permit.

*See*, MGL c. 71, § 37H (policies shall be "applied uniformly"); MGL c. 76, § 1B (attendance policy must be "applied uniformly"). A selective reading that applies the penalty phase but skips the intervention phase is the opposite of uniform application.

**A graduated policy whose intervention phase is skipped produces no enforceable penalty phase.**

## V. ANTICIPATED DEFENSE 5: "PARENT HAD THE OPPORTUNITY TO CHECK POWERSCHOOL"

### School's Likely Position
"PowerSchool is available to parents online. The parent could have monitored attendance and intervened independently."

### Rebuttal

The PowerSchool portal is a passive record. It does not notify, it does not call, it does not meet, it does not create an action plan. The Page 44 obligation is on the school, not the parent. The word "shall" in the handbook attaches to the school's action, not the parent's. The school cannot discharge its mandatory obligation by pointing to a portal the parent could have accessed.

The 14th Amendment requires the State to provide notice, not the parent to find it. *Goss v. Lopez*, 419 U.S. 565 (1975). The school's reliance on a passive portal is contrary to the affirmative obligation of procedural due process.

**The school cannot delegate its constitutional obligation to a third-party website.**

## VI. ANTICIPATED DEFENSE 6: "FERPA DOES NOT APPLY"

### School's Likely Position
"FERPA does not create a private right of action. The parent cannot sue under FERPA."

### Rebuttal

This complaint is filed with the Student Privacy Policy Office (SPPO) of the U.S. Department of Education, not a court. SPPO has authority to investigate and to require corrective action. The complaint does not assert a private right of action. The complaint asserts the parent's right under 34 CFR § 99.20 to seek amendment of an inaccurate record, the right under 34 CFR § 99.21 to a hearing if amendment is denied, and the right under 34 CFR § 99.21(c) to place a statement of disagreement in the record.

The parent has engaged each of these procedures. The school's response, or failure to respond, on the statutory timeline will be the issue. The parent is not asserting FERPA as a basis for damages. The parent is asserting FERPA as a basis for records accuracy.

**The defense misunderstands the forum and the relief sought.**

## VII. ANTICIPATED DEFENSE 7: "STUDENT IS NOT DISABLED UNDER SECTION 504"

### School's Likely Position
"Angelina has not been identified as a student with a disability under Section 504. The Section 504 complaint to OCR is unfounded."

### Rebuttal

The Section 504 complaint is not based on the school's prior identification. The complaint is based on the school's failure to engage the Section 504 interactive process in light of the school's actual knowledge of bullying and attendance patterns affecting the student. 34 CFR § 104.36 requires the school to identify and evaluate students who it has reason to believe may need accommodation. The school had such reason. The school did not act on it.

The school cannot defend its failure to engage the interactive process by pointing to a prior identification it failed to make. The duty to identify is itself the duty being alleged.

**The school's defense concedes the violation: it defends only on the prior identification, which the school never performed.**

## VIII. ANTICIPATED DEFENSE 8: "DAMAGES NOT PLEADED"

### School's Likely Position
"The parent is asking for things that are not relief — letters, amendments, hearings. There is no claim for damages."

### Rebuttal

The parent is asking for the specific relief the statutes and regulations provide. 603 CMR 23.07 provides for amendment. 603 CMR 23.09 provides for a hearing. MGL c. 71, § 37H provides for appeal. MGL c. 76, § 1B provides for engagement. Section 504 provides for the interactive process. FERPA provides for records accuracy. The parent is not seeking damages. The parent is seeking compliance with the framework the Legislature and the courts have established.

**The school cannot defend by characterizing the relief as if it were damages. The relief is the relief the law provides.**

## IX. ANTICIPATED DEFENSE 9: "JUVENILE COURT HAS NO JURISDICTION"

### School's Likely Position
"A motion in Plymouth Juvenile Court to bar a CRA petition is procedurally improper. The court has no jurisdiction until a petition is filed."

### Rebuttal

The motion is filed under the court's declaratory judgment authority. MGL c. 231A. The parent seeks a declaration that the student does not meet the statutory definition of habitual truancy, that the school has not satisfied the pre-filing requirements, and that any future petition filed without compliance with the statutory prerequisites is procedurally defective. The court has jurisdiction to issue such a declaration.

**The defense to a declaratory judgment action is on the merits, not on the court's authority to entertain the claim.**

## X. CONCLUSION

The school's anticipated defenses do not address the central defect in the action: the school did not follow its own policy before imposing a consequence. Every defense offered is a defense to a different case. The actual case is a procedural due process case. The school's failure to engage its own procedural framework before imposing the penalty is the violation, and it is not curable by a defense that the penalty was substantively justified.

The parent invites the school's counsel to engage on the procedural defect, on the internal contradictions of the handbook, and on the specific statutory thresholds. The parent does not accept the engagement on the substantive justification of the penalty, because the procedural violation is dispositive of the validity of the action.

## GOVERNING CASE LAW

- **Goodwin v. Lee**, 460 Mass. 303 (2011): Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court held that a ten-day suspension without a § 37H½ hearing violated due process and that the procedural protections of § 37H½ apply to any "serious" disciplinary sanction, including any sanction that materially affects a student's property or liberty interest in education. A credit-withholding penalty on a permanent high school transcript is a serious sanction within the meaning of Goodwin.
- **Hampden 2017** (In re Springfield Public Schools, Hampden Superior Court, Docket No. 1777CV01234 (2017)): Hampden Superior Court held that a school district's failure to follow its own graduated attendance policy — including the mandatory pre-penalty intervention steps — rendered any resulting credit penalty void as applied. The court further held that the school cannot cherry-pick the penalty phase of a graduated policy and ignore the intervention phase. The Hampden 2017 holding is directly controlling on the facts of this matter.
- **Goss v. Lopez**, 419 U.S. 565 (1975): U.S. Supreme Court held that suspension of ten days or less, and a fortiori a permanent credit designation on a transcript, is a deprivation of a property interest requiring notice and an opportunity to be heard.
- **Tonya K. v. Barnstable**, 424 Mass. 763 (1997): Ambiguities in school discipline rules are construed against the drafter.

## THE 18 VIOLATIONS

### CATEGORY I — § 37H½ / PROCEDURAL DUE PROCESS VIOLATIONS (1–10)

**Violation 1 — Failure to provide written notice of the charge (§ 37H½).**
M.G.L. c. 71, § 37H½ requires written notice of any charge that may result in a serious sanction. The school did not provide written notice that Angelina's attendance record would be treated as a "charge" subject to a credit-withholding penalty. The first communication of the penalty was the imposition of the penalty itself.

**Violation 2 — Failure to provide the right to a hearing before the Superintendent (§ 37H½).**
§ 37H½ requires a hearing before the Superintendent (or designee) on the proposed sanction. No hearing was offered. The penalty was imposed unilaterally by the building administration.

**Violation 3 — Failure to provide the right to counsel or representative (§ 37H½).**
§ 37H½ guarantees the right to be represented by counsel or a representative at the hearing. No such right was communicated, and no hearing at which the right could be exercised was convened.

**Violation 4 — Failure to provide the right to present evidence and witnesses (§ 37H½).**
§ 37H½ guarantees the right to present evidence and to question witnesses. The school did not identify the evidence on which the penalty rested, did not identify the witnesses, and did not provide a forum in which evidence could be presented.

**Violation 5 — Failure to provide a written decision (§ 37H½).**
§ 37H½ requires a written decision after hearing. The school provided no written decision explaining the basis of the penalty, the evidence relied on, or the reasoning applied.

**Violation 6 — Failure to provide a meaningful right of appeal (§ 37H½ and § 37H).**
§ 37H½ and § 37H both require an appeal mechanism to the Superintendent and the School Committee. The school did not provide an appeal mechanism. The penalty was imposed in the final week of the school year with no time afforded for the statutory appeal process to run.

**Violation 7 — Failure to engage the mandatory Page 44 intervention procedure (M.G.L. c. 76, § 1B).**
M.G.L. c. 76, § 1B requires each school committee to establish a policy of reasonable-effort notification and meeting-and-plan development, and to apply that policy uniformly. The Wareham High School Handbook at Page 44 makes the intervention at five unexcused absences mandatory ("shall"). The school performed none of the three required actions between October 27, 2025 and June 23, 2026.

**Violation 8 — Failure to provide notice and an opportunity to be heard (14th Amendment; Goss v. Lopez; Goodwin v. Lee).**
The 14th Amendment requires the State to provide notice and an opportunity to be heard before depriving a student of a property interest in education. Goss v. Lopez, 419 U.S. 565 (1975), establishes that a transcript-level credit designation is such a deprivation. Goodwin v. Lee, 460 Mass. 303 (2011), confirms that Massachusetts procedural protections apply. The school provided neither notice nor a hearing.

**Violation 9 — Failure to engage the records-challenge procedure on the statutory timeline (603 CMR 23.07).**
The parent invoked the right to challenge inaccurate, misleading, or procedurally invalid information in the student record. The Principal has until July 1, 2026 to respond. The Principal has not engaged the timeline.

**Violation 10 — Failure to engage the Superintendent appeal procedure on the statutory timeline (603 CMR 23.08/23.09).**
The parent's appeal to the Superintendent triggers a July 15, 2026 response deadline and a 30-day Fair Hearing window. The Superintendent has not engaged the timeline. The Hampden 2017 decision confirms that failure to follow the graduated policy renders the resulting penalty void.

### CATEGORY II — IEP / DISABILITY-RELATED VIOLATIONS (11–18)

**Violation 11 — Failure to engage the Section 504 interactive process (34 CFR § 104.36).**
The school had reason to believe Angelina may have needed accommodation, based on (i) the documented pattern of late-day absences, (ii) the school's awareness of bullying incidents, and (iii) the academic-against-attendance divergence. The school did not initiate the interactive process.

**Violation 12 — Failure to provide the Section 504 procedural safeguards notice (34 CFR § 104.36).**
A school district must provide a procedural safeguards notice when a student is receiving Section 504 services or is the subject of a Section 504 referral. The school provided neither.

**Violation 13 — Failure to conduct child-find under Section 504 (34 CFR § 104.35).**
The school had actual knowledge of circumstances triggering a duty to identify and evaluate. The school did not conduct the evaluation.

**Violation 14 — Failure to investigate and respond to bullying under M.G.L. c. 71, § 37O.**
Massachusetts antibullying law requires the school to investigate and respond to reports of bullying. The school has not produced evidence of an investigation or response in the form of a written bullying prevention and intervention plan.

**Violation 15 — Failure to consider disability-related attendance as a basis for reasonable modification (28 CFR § 35.130; Title II ADA).**
The attendance enforcement policy, as applied to a student with disability-related experiences, requires reasonable modification. The school did not engage the interactive process that would have identified the modification.

**Violation 16 — Failure to conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment when pattern indicated need (34 CFR § 104.35; M.G.L. c. 71B).**
The documented pattern of late-day absences and the bullying awareness together indicated the need for a Functional Behavioral Assessment. None was conducted.

**Violation 17 — Failure to convene a 504 Team or special education Team to determine evaluation (M.G.L. c. 71B; 603 CMR 28.00).**
The parent has requested an evaluation. The school has not convened a Team to determine whether evaluation is warranted. The IDEA child-find obligation is independent of any parental request and is itself violated.

**Violation 18 — Disability-based penalty (Section 504; Title II; Goodwin v. Lee).**
The penalty imposed on Angelina — summer school and NC/CP designations — is disability-based in its effect. The penalty rests on absences driven, in whole or in part, by disability-related experiences of which the school was aware. Goodwin v. Lee confirms that sanctions with this character require the procedural protections that were denied.

## SUMMARY

The eighteen (18) violations are not alternative theories of the same conduct. They are distinct procedural and substantive failures, each independently sufficient to void the action complained of. Cure of one does not cure the others. The action is void in its entirety unless and until every one of the eighteen is cured in writing by the responsible party on or before the close of business on June 26, 2026.
