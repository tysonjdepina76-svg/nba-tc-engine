# CHAPTER 12 — JOHN STANFA

*The man who built walls. The wisemen who taught me how to fall.*

---

John Stanfa was power.

I'd known that before I got here. Everyone inside knew that. Stanfa was the name that men whispered when they talked about who ran things—not just this facility, but the whole network, the whole operation that stretched across institutions and states and years. Stanfa had been doing time since before I was born. Stanfa had built an empire inside the walls. Walls that he maintained. Walls that kept people in line. Walls that protected what was his.

And then the wisemen started dying.

---

2018. First one gone.

I watched Stanfa's face when he heard the news. That's what I remember most. Not the details of who died or how or why—the details didn't matter as much as the way his face changed when the information came through.

The armor cracked.

Not all the way. Not completely. But enough. Enough to see the man under the title. Enough to see the grief underneath the power.

Stanfa was supposed to be untouchable. That was the whole point of him. That was what his power meant—not just control over the population, but immunity from the things that break ordinary men. He wasn't supposed to cry. He wasn't supposed to look tired. He wasn't supposed to look like someone whose people had been taken from him.

But grief doesn't care about armor. Grief doesn't care about walls. Grief finds the cracks and it gets in.

---

2019. Second one gone.

The crack in Stanfa's face became a fracture. Then a shatter.

I'd never seen a man come apart like that. Not on the street. Not in any of the facilities I'd been in. I'd seen men get tough, seen men get hard, seen men bury their grief so deep they'd convinced themselves it wasn't there. But I'd never watched a man who had built his entire identity around invincibility watch that identity fall apart in front of him.

Two wisemen dead. Both of them gone within a year. Both of them losses that couldn't be replaced.

Stanfa's face after the second one—it was the face of a man who understood something he'd spent his whole life trying to outrun. That power doesn't protect you from loss. That control doesn't keep grief from finding you. That the walls you build to keep everyone else out also keep you trapped inside with your own pain.

I saw myself in him.

I'd spent my whole life building walls too. Different walls. Not an empire inside a prison—just a shell around myself, a hardness that kept everyone out, an armor that I thought was keeping me alive. But watching Stanfa grieve, watching the walls collapse on him, I understood something I hadn't understood before:

The armor doesn't protect you from grief. The armor just makes grief heavier when it finally arrives.

---

2020. Two left.

By the time the second wiseman was gone, two others remained. Two men still standing. Two men who'd watched their brothers fall and were still holding on.

And Stanfa was defeated. Not in the legal sense. Not in the sense of his operation or his power or his control. He was defeated in the way that matters—the way that makes you wake up every morning and wonder why you're still here.

Two wisemen dead. Two still breathing. And Stanfa was still locked up, still inside, still watching the years stack up while the people he'd built his kingdom for were six feet underground.

I watched him and I thought: that could be me.

Not the dying. The losing.

I had people too. I'd lost people too—Tanisha when I was four, my grandfather, my great-grandmother, Scotty Wat in 1996 while I was locked up. I'd lost Miguel and Paul G. when I was twelve, learning the lesson that the streets take people you love.

But I'd never finished grieving any of them. I'd just kept moving. Kept running. Kept putting one foot in front of the other because stopping meant feeling, and feeling meant breaking, and breaking meant I might not get back up.

Stanfa didn't get back up. The grief finished him.

And I watched, and I understood: I was next. If I didn't do something different. If I didn't find a way to feel the grief instead of running from it. If I kept letting the armor calcify around everything I'd never finished mourning.

I was going to end up like him. Broken. Defeated. A cautionary tale that the system could point to and say: see, we told you he was nothing. See, we told you he'd die in here.

Two dead. Two left. And me—the potential fifth.

The cell was the same. The years were the same. The loss was the same.

The only difference was what I decided to do with it.
