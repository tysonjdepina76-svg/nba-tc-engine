# CHAPTER 14 — I AM NOT DYING IN HERE

*The decision. The three words that changed everything.*

---

The cell was eight feet by ten feet. Concrete walls. Concrete floor. A steel bunk. A steel toilet. A small window that let in a sliver of light and nothing else. I'd been in cells like this before — the system had given me plenty of practice — but this time was different.

This time I was forty years old.

This time I had been looking at thirty years. Thirty to life. The number they put in front of me when they indicted me the second time. Thirty years. Three hundred and sixty months. A number so large it didn't feel real until you tried to break it down into days, into hours, into the specific moments of waking up in this cell and going to sleep in this cell and doing it again and again and again until you were old enough that nobody would recognize you anymore.

And in that cell, in that moment, something changed.

Not the circumstances. The case was still there. The years were still stacking. The feds still had their evidence, their witnesses, their narrative. Everything outside my cell was exactly the same.

But inside — something shifted.

---

I had been saying it in my head for a while. Not consciously. Not as a decision. Just as a thought that would surface sometimes in the middle of the night when I couldn't sleep, in the early morning when the cell was quiet and the weight of the years pressed down on my chest.

I'm going to die in here.

It wasn't self-pity. It wasn't giving up. It was arithmetic. I had been doing the math in my head: if I got thirty years, I would be seventy when I got out. If I got twenty-five, I would be sixty-five. Either way, by the time I walked out of a prison — if I walked out at all — I would be old. Worn out. Used up. The streets would have moved on without me. The world would have changed in ways I couldn't imagine. My children would have grown up without me. My mother might be gone.

The math said I was going to die inside these walls.

And then one night — I remember the exact moment, the exact feeling, the exact shift — I said it differently.

---

I am not dying in here.

Three words. Different words. Different weight. Different meaning.

Not a statement of fact. Not arithmetic. A decision.

I am not dying in here.

Not a wish. Wishes are passive. Wishes are hoping something happens to you. Wishes are waiting for the world to change in ways you can't control.

This was a decision. This was active. This was me looking at every piece of evidence that said I was going to die in this cell and saying: no. Not me. Not this. Not today.

I am not dying in here.

And then the fear came.

---

That's the thing nobody tells you about deciding to live.

It's scarier than deciding to die.

When you're in a cell and you believe you're going to die there — when you've accepted the arithmetic, done the math, made peace with the number — there's a certain calm in it. A resignation. A way of being that doesn't require anything from you because the outcome is already determined.

But when you decide to live — really decide, the kind of decision that changes the direction of everything — suddenly you have to be someone who lives. You have to become the person who survives. You have to start doing the things that a living person does: hoping, planning, investing in a future that might not come, believing in a version of yourself you haven't been in decades.

The fear was: what if I fail?

What if I decide to live and I don't make it? What if I let myself hope and then the hope gets crushed? What if I try and the system breaks me anyway?

The fear was: what if I live and I'm still not enough?

What if I walk out of here at sixty-five and I'm still the same person I was at forty? Still hard. Still armored. Still carrying everything I never dealt with? What if freedom doesn't change me? What if I'm just an old criminal instead of a transformed one?

The fear was real. The fear was huge. The fear was the first real thing I had let myself feel in years.

And I felt it anyway.

---

I am not dying in here.

I wrote it down. First on paper — a scrap I found somewhere, a corner of something I shouldn't have had. Then in my head. Then as a mantra. Then as a compass. Every morning I would wake up in that cell and I would say it:

I am not dying in here.

Not because it was true. Not because I knew I would make it. Not because I had any evidence that things would be different.

Because I decided it was true. Because I decided things would be different. Because I decided that this cell — this case — these years — would not be the end of me.

They would be the beginning.

---

The decision was the first step.

The work came next.

But the decision — the moment I looked at thirty to life and said no, the moment I let myself feel the fear and move forward anyway, the moment I chose living over surviving — that was the pivot point. That was the moment everything changed.

I am not dying in here.

Three words that became my lifeline. Three words that I held onto in the darkest nights, in the loneliest hours, in the moments when every piece of evidence said I was done.

I held onto them like a rope.

And I started climbing.
