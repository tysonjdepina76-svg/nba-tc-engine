# CHAPTER 9B — THE CARTEL

*Before the federal case. El Paso. Juarez. The school of how the world works.*

---

Before the federal case collapsed. Before the 2005 indictment. Before the three hundred and sixty months that almost ended me.

That's when the cartel connection happened.

Shareek called. My cousin. Blood. Someone I'd known my whole life, who'd grown up in the same neighborhood, who'd learned the same lessons I had about what the streets owed you and what they took. Shareek had a line. A real one. Connected to the source. And he was offering me in.

I should have said no.

I knew that even then. Even as I was getting on the plane, even as I was driving to El Paso, even as I was crossing into Juarez to meet people whose names I would never know and whose lives would never cross mine except in the specific and narrow way of product and money—I knew I should have stopped.

But I didn't.

---

The trips were specific. Half-kilo runs. The product coming from Mexico, moving through the pipeline, ending up in hands I'd never see and lives I'd never touch except in the abstract calculus of supply and demand.

I understood what I was doing. I wasn't naive. I wasn't confused about the nature of the business.

Somebody was going to use this whether I moved it or not. So I might as well be the one who gets paid.

Beck was there. The network was tight, connected, professional in the way that only the people who've been doing this for decades can be professional. We moved product. We moved money. We moved through border towns and border crossings and hotels and safe houses with the kind of ease that comes from practice.

Beck would later get caught up in the federal case—that case from 2005, the one that almost took me down. But before all that, before the indictment, before any of it—we were running together. Learning. Moving. Getting deeper into a world that was bigger than anything I'd imagined.

---

Shareek. Beck. Snack. El Paso. Juarez. The half-kilo runs.

The education was thorough. I learned how the international drug trade worked at the street level. Where it came from. How it moved. Who got paid and how much. The entire ecosystem of a business that governments worldwide had spent decades trying to eradicate.

I learned who the players were. I learned the language. I learned the risks—the specific, concrete risks of moving product across borders, of meeting people you couldn't trust, of handling money that came with blood on it.

I learned that the feds were always watching. That the heat was always on. That the next run could be the one that finally caught up.

And I kept running anyway.

---

By the time the federal case came—the 2005 indictment, the twenty-one people, the three hundred and sixty months—I understood exactly what I was facing. I'd been inside that world. I'd touched those edges. I knew how close I'd come to something worse, how easily the next run could have been the one that put me under for good.

The case collapsed. I walked.

But the education stayed with me. And the understanding: I had been closer to the edge than I ever let myself believe.
