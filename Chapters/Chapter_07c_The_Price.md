# CHAPTER 7C — THE PRICE

*1996. 1998. Scotty Wat. Shawn G. Uncle Joey. The war takes its toll.*

---

1996. Scotty Wat.

I was locked up when it happened. Twenty years old, doing time for the guns and the drugs, sitting in a cell somewhere between New Bedford and the rest of my life—and Scotty Wat was out there dying. They said it happened fast. They said he didn't suffer. They said a lot of things that were supposed to make it easier to hear, but nothing makes it easier when you're locked up and somebody you love is gone and you can't even get to the funeral.

Scotty Wat was steady. Scotty Wat was the one who showed up. When everyone else was running and hiding and posturing, Scotty Wat was the one who put his neck out for you. That's a rare thing in the West End. That's a rare thing anywhere.

And now he was dead. And I was in a cell. And the war was still going.

---

1998. Uncle Joey.

My mother's brother. My favorite uncle. The one who actually gave a damn—the one who had done time himself, who understood what it was like to be on the inside looking out, who had gotten his life together on the other side. Uncle Joey was proof that you could go in and come out and build something real. He had a house. He had a routine. He had the kind of quiet stability that made you believe change was possible.

He was going to let me parole to his house.

That's what he told me. That's what we talked about when I was locked up and he was out there living the life I'd been trying to get to for years. He was going to be there when I got out. He was going to help me figure it out. He was going to show me the way.

And then 1998 happened.

My uncle Joey died. And the proof that change was possible died with him.

I don't know what happened. I don't know how or why or what went wrong in the specific details that would make it make sense. What I know is this: one day he was alive and planning for my release, and the next day he was gone, and I was still in a cell, and the future I'd been looking forward to—the future that had a shape now, a destination, a person who believed in me—had collapsed into nothing.

I had to grieve him from inside a prison cell. I had to add his name to the list of people I loved and lost and couldn't say goodbye to. I had to keep moving because that's what you do when you're locked up. You don't get to take time off. You don't get to break down in the middle of the day and put yourself back together. You lock it away and you keep your face straight and you do your time.

Uncle Joey taught me something before he died: he taught me that people who get their lives together can still lose them. That getting out and staying out is a different skill than getting in. That the world doesn't care how much time you've done or how hard you've worked or how much you want to be different—it can still take you anyway.

He also taught me something else: he taught me that I could be like him. That there was a version of me that made it. That the armor I'd built wasn't the only version of Tyson that was possible.

His death didn't prove that. His life did.

And then his death took that proof away.

---

1998. Shawn G.

Another one. The war kept collecting names. The list kept growing. Miguel. Paul G. Scotty Wat. Shawn G. And I was still here—still locked up, still doing time, still adding names to the list in my head without knowing if I'd ever be able to stop counting.

Shawn G. was different from the others. Shawn G. was family. Shawn G. was the kind of connection that hit different—when it's your cousin, when it's your blood, when the loss moves from the abstract to the personal.

I remember when he used to come around. I remember his laugh. I remember the way he moved through the world like he wasn't afraid of anything, like the streets were just a playground and not a trap that was slowly closing.

By 1998, I knew better.

The streets don't let you be a kid for long. The streets don't care about your plans. The streets will take your uncle, your cousin, your best friend, and keep right on moving.

I was learning the lesson again. I was going to keep learning it for the rest of my life.

---

How many names can you count before you stop counting?

I never stopped. I just learned to carry them differently.
