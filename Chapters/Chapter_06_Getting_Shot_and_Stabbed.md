# CHAPTER 6 — GETTING SHOT AND STABBED

*Three months apart. The escalation.*

---

January 18, 2001. 

That's a date I remember. Not because I wanted to remember it—but because my body remembers things my mind tries to forget. The cold of that night. The way the air hit different in the winter. The sound—pop, crack—that my brain wouldn't process for the first few seconds while everything caught up with the reality that I was on the ground and there was blood and I didn't understand what happened.

That's the thing about getting shot—you rarely see it coming. You don't get the dramatic moment where you see the other guy raise the gun and have time to react, run, dive behind cover. You hear a sound and then you're done.

The bullet went through my side. Not the chest—would've been over if it went through the chest. Through the side, through the soft tissue, through the part of you that's solid but not vital. Painful as hell, but survivable. The kind of wound that puts you in the hospital for a few weeks and leaves a scar you can show people later to prove you survived something.

I was twenty-four years old. About to turn twenty-five in April. Old enough to know better. Young enough to think I was invincible.

I showed the scar a few times. Not to brag—okay, maybe a little to brag—but to prove something to myself. To prove I was hard enough. That I'd taken a hit and gotten back up. That the streets had tried to take me out and I'd said no.

What I didn't show anyone was the aftermath. The hours in the hospital when the morphine wore off and the pain came back and I had nothing to do but lie there and think about what had happened. The realization that I could have died. That if the bullet had gone two inches to the left, or if the guy who'd shot me had aimed better, or if nobody had found me in time—I would've been another name people said in the past tense.

Miguel. Paul G. Scotty Wat. Tyson.

The thought should've scared me. Any normal person would've looked at that moment—the blood, the hospital, the scar—and decided to change something. Find a different way. Get out before the next one found you.

But I wasn't normal. The system had made sure of that. By twenty-four, I was so deep into the armor that I'd forgotten there was anything underneath it. There was no Tyson underneath the armor. There was only the hardness. Only the code. Only the next move.

Three months later, in April 2001, I got stabbed.

---

The stabbing was different. More intimate. Closer.

A gun is distance. You don't have to see the other person's face when you pull the trigger. You don't have to watch their expression change when the bullet hits. You don't have to stand there and know—really know—that you put a piece of metal into another human being's body.

A knife requires proximity. A knife requires presence. When you stab someone, you're close enough to see their eyes. Close enough to see recognition, fear, rage. Close enough to smell them—sweat, breath, the specific smell of another body that knows it's about to be damaged.

The guy who stabbed me was bigger than me. Older. He had something I wanted—respect, territory, I don't even remember what the beef was about anymore—and when we got into it, he pulled a knife and he didn't hesitate.

The blade went into my arm. Not deep—deflected by bone, by the way my body twisted when I saw the knife coming—but deep enough to require stitches. Deep enough to add another scar to the collection. Deep enough to remind me that the streets don't let you win.

I stabbed him back. Of course I did. You don't get stabbed and not retaliate in the West End. That's not how it works. The code says an eye for an eye, a stab for a stab, a life for a life. Balance. Justice. Whatever word helps you sleep at night when you can't stop shaking from the adrenaline wearing off.

But here's what I remember most about those three months:

It didn't scare me.

Getting shot should terrify you. Three months later, getting stabbed should convince you that the next one might be the one that kills you. Any rational person would look at that pattern—gun, knife, escalating violence—and understand it was only a matter of time before one of them ended the story permanently.

I wasn't rational. I hadn't been rational since I was four years old and found Tanisha cold in her crib. The part of my brain that knew fear, that registered danger, that told normal people to run—that part had been cut out so long ago I didn't even know it was missing.

What I felt instead was confirmation.

The shot proved I was a target. The stab proved I could take a hit. And if I could take a hit and keep standing, I was harder than the people trying to take me out. I was willing to do what they weren't. That was the edge. That was what set me apart.

The truth was simpler and sadder than the story I told myself:

I didn't fear death because I'd never learned to value life.

Tanisha died when I was four. My grandfather died. My great-grandmother died. Miguel and Paul G. died. The people I loved disappeared, and nobody helped me understand why. Nobody told me it was okay to miss them, to cry, to rage against the unfairness of it all. I was supposed to be a boy. Boys don't break. Boys keep moving. Boys don't ask why.

So I stopped asking. I stopped feeling. And by the time I was twenty-five, sitting in a hospital bed with a hole in my side and another scar on my arm, I thought the numbness was strength.

I didn't know I was just numb because the grief had nowhere to go.
