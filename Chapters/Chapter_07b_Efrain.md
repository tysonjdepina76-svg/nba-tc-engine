# CHAPTER 7B — EFRAIN

*The water tower. Age 15. The moment before the war.*

---

I was fifteen the first time I almost died.

Not from the gunshots that would come later. Not from the stabbings. Not from the years inside that would teach me exactly how fragile the body is and exactly how much punishment it can take. No—this was different. This was a choice. Mine. Made in a moment when the weight got so heavy that I couldn't see over it anymore.

The water tower sat on the edge of the West End like a sentinel, a metal giant that overlooked the whole neighborhood. We'd climb it sometimes—not often, not casually, but when we needed to think, or hide, or feel like we were above everything for a little while. From up there you could see the whole block. The corners we ran. The spaces we ruled. The world that was ours and the world that wasn't.

I went up alone.

I don't remember the exact day anymore. I don't remember what triggered it—whether it was the memory of Tanisha still pressing on my chest like a weight I couldn't lift, or Miguel and Paul G. still fresh in my mind, or just the accumulated weight of four years since DYS when nothing had gotten better and the armor was getting thicker and I couldn't remember what it felt like to be anything other than hard. I went up there and I stood on the edge and I looked down and I thought: I could end this.

The grief was so heavy.

That's what I remember most. Not the anger. Not the rage that I'd learned to use as fuel. The grief—the actual, underneath, bone-deep grief of a boy who'd lost too much too fast and never been allowed to feel it. Tanisha cold in her crib. My grandfather gone. My great-grandmother gone. Miguel and Paul G. gone. All of them gone and I was still here and nobody had ever told me it was okay to miss them.

I stood on the edge.

And then I stepped back.

I don't know why. I don't have a dramatic story about a voice or a vision or a sudden understanding of my purpose. I just stepped back. Maybe it was the code—the part of me that had already decided I was too hard to kill, that I would survive because that's what I did. Maybe it was something else. Maybe it was just survival instinct kicking in before the conscious thought.

I climbed down.

I went back to the block. Back to the corner. Back to the life that was trying to kill me and that I was trying to kill back.

But I remember standing on that tower. I remember the view. I remember thinking that if I let myself feel how heavy it was—the grief, the loneliness, the understanding that nobody was coming to help me—I wouldn't survive it.

So I didn't feel it.

I buried it. Locked it away. Built another layer of armor on top of the grief I'd already buried.

And I kept moving.
