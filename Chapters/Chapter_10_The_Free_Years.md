# CHAPTER 10 — THE FREE YEARS

*August 2011–November 2016. Five years of borrowed time.*

---

Five years.

That's what I had between August 2011 and November 2016. Five years of walking around free, breathing fresh air, sleeping in my own bed—technically. Five years that most people would give anything for.

Five years that I spent mostly the same way I'd spent the twenty years before.

Running.

Hustling.

Living the life I'd always lived because I didn't know any other way to live.

People ask me now—what did freedom feel like? After almost doing thirty to life? After watching years disappear inside while the federal case collapsed, while I waited in Plymouth County, while Coolidge House became my address and the ankle monitor became my shadow?

The honest answer is: it felt like nothing. It felt like the baseline. It felt like the default setting I always went back to because I'd never learned how to be anything else.

---

I tried to stop. That's the thing people don't understand.

Every time I walked out of a courthouse, a jail, a DYS facility—I tried. I went home. I spent time with my mother, with my kids when they were little, with the people who loved me and wanted something different for me. I felt the desire to change—genuine desire, the kind that wakes you up at 3 AM and makes you stare at the ceiling wondering why you keep making the same choices.

But desire without a plan is just wishing. And I didn't have a plan. Nobody gave me a plan. Nobody sat me down and said here's how you build a life that's different, here's how you make money legally, here's how you survive without the streets.

The streets had taught me everything. How to read a situation. How to protect myself. How to make quick money. How to read people—whose trustworthy, whose not, whose full of shit, whose dangerous. Those skills were useful on the block. They weren't useless everywhere. But they weren't enough to build a life with.

So I drifted.

Back to what I knew. Back to the money. Back to the people who spoke the same language, who operated the same way, who didn't ask questions because the answers would complicate things.

---

The children.

My daughter was born in 1999—I was twenty-three, still locked up, still trying to figure out how to be a father from inside a cell. My son came in 2001—the same year I got shot and stabbed, the same year I was supposed to be turning my life around. My youngest came later, in 2004—the year of Rivet Street, the year the feds almost took me down.

Three children. Three reasons to be different. Three people who needed me to be something other than what I'd always been.

And I tried. I wanted to be there. I wanted to be present. But the streets don't let you be present—the streets demand your attention, your time, your energy, your soul. Every visit to the prison was a reminder of what I couldn't give. Every phone call home was a reminder of the distance between who I was and who I wanted to be.

When I finally got out for good—when the second case finally closed and I knew the number I was facing—I had missed most of their lives. My daughter was already twenty-five. My son was twenty-three. My youngest was twenty.

They'd grown up without me. They'd learned to be a family without me. And I was proud of them—fiercely proud—but I also knew what my absence had cost. I knew what they hadn't had that they deserved.

That's the part of prison nobody talks about. The part that stays with you after the gates close behind you. The years you can't get back. The birthdays you missed. The moments that happened without you, that you only hear about secondhand, that shaped the people your children became while you weren't watching.

---

August 2011 to November 2016. Five years.

Five years to think about what almost happened. Five years to understand that the next time might not have the same ending. Five years to prepare—for the case, for the time, for the life I'd have to build inside if this was all I had left.

Five years that I wasted.

That's the truth. I look back at those years now—at the decisions I made, the opportunities I had, the people I could have been instead of the person I was—and I understand that I was just marking time. Not living. Not growing. Not changing. Just existing in the space between the case that almost killed me and the case that actually did.

The second case wouldn't come for another five years. November 2016. But when it came, it came harder, deeper, with more weight than the first.

And this time, the government wouldn't let go.
