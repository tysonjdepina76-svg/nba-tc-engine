# CHAPTER 9 — THE FEDERAL BUST

*November 2005. 360 months. The case that almost ended me.*

---

November 2005. I was twenty-nine years old.

The indictment came down like a hammer. Twenty-one people from my network—my operation, my connections, my life—swept up in a single day. The government alleged twelve and a half kilos of cocaine. Over one hundred grams of black tar heroin. A kilo of crack. Sixteen guns including an AK-47.

And me. One count. A single heroin count. But a heroin count that carried a label: career offender. Thirty years to life. Three hundred and sixty months. The rest of my natural life in a cell.

I was held without bail.

That was the first thing I understood: I wasn't going home. Not tomorrow. Not next week. The feds had me and they weren't letting go until they got what they wanted—a conviction, a sentence, a man boxed in for the rest of his natural life.

Plymouth County. That's where I went. November 2005, sitting in a cell, waiting for lawyers and hearings and the machinery of justice to grind me up. The lawyers explained the numbers. What thirty to life looked like in concrete terms. I'd be sixty when I got out—if I survived. If the prison system didn't take me first.

The government's theory was simple: the quantity—between one hundred and four hundred grams of heroin—triggered the career offender enhancement. I'd been through the system before. I fit the profile. And the law didn't care about context, about how I'd ended up here, about the fourteen-year-old boy who'd been told he was nothing and had spent fifteen years proving everyone right.

They weren't entirely wrong. I wasn't innocent. I'd done the things they said I'd done. I'd been part of the network. I'd moved product, handled money, made decisions that destroyed lives.

September 2008. Three years inside. Transferred to Wyatt. Then MDC Brooklyn.

And then—weeks before trial—the case collapsed.

The government couldn't prove the quantity. The evidence didn't support the number. The career offender enhancement fell apart, and with it, their entire theory of the case.

I walked out of MDC Brooklyn a free man.

November 2005 to September 2008. Almost three years inside. Three years of my life taken by a case that almost took the rest.

But I was out. Twenty-nine going on thirty-two. Free for the first time in years with nothing but the ghost of three hundred and sixty months and the understanding that the next time wouldn't have the same ending.

The cartel connection was part of that life—years of understanding how the international drug trade worked at the street level. The education I couldn't get anywhere else. The network I was part of before the feds came knocking.

And now I was back on the outside with all of it still in my head.
