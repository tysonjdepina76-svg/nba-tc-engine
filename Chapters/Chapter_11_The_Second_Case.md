# CHAPTER 11 — THE SECOND CASE

*November 2016. Age 40. The cell that finally held.*

---

November 2016. Forty years old.

In a cell that felt like every cell I'd ever been in and worse than all of them at the same time.

The difference was the weight.

When I went into DYS at fourteen, I was a kid. I didn't understand what was happening, not really. The armor was forming but it wasn't finished. There was still something in me that believed in fairness—that if I was good enough, smart enough, tough enough, I could make it through. That the system was something you could survive if you just played it right.

When I got out of DYS at sixteen, I went right back to the streets. Got locked up again in 1999 on a parole violation. Scotty Wat—my guy, my people—got killed in 1996 while I was locked up. The streets had taken him. The game had taken him. And I was sitting in a cell when it happened, unable to do anything, unable to protect him, unable to be there.

I got out again. And again. And again. The cycle never stopped.

The 2005 case almost ended me. Twenty-one people arrested. Twelve and a half kilos of cocaine. Over a hundred grams of black tar heroin. A kilo of crack. Sixteen guns including an AK-47. I was facing thirty to life as a career offender. Three hundred and sixty months. A month from trial.

And then—the government couldn't prove the quantity. The case collapsed. I walked.

Nine years of freedom. And then November 2016.

---

Shareek was my cousin.

That's who they got me with. Blood. Family. The one person I thought I could trust, the one person I let in close enough to be standing right there when the feds came.

Conspiracy. One hundred to four hundred grams of heroin and fentanyl. Not the quantity I almost got killed on in 2005. Not a technicality this time. Not a collapse. Just the facts: I was connected, I was moving product, and this time they had enough to prove it.

The armor clicked off in an instant.

I remember the moment. Standing in the cell. Looking at the walls. Understanding: this is it. This is the moment I always knew was coming. Not if—when.

But this time was different.

In 2005, I was twenty-nine. I still thought I was invincible. I still thought I'd figure a way out. The armor was still on. I could still outrun it, outsmart it, survive it the way I'd survived everything else.

In 2016, at forty years old, I looked at the walls and I understood: there was no way out. No technicality. No collapse. No clever lawyer and no lucky break. Just years. Decades. The rest of my life, maybe.

And underneath that understanding came something I hadn't felt in decades.

Fear.

Not the fear of getting caught. Not the fear of consequences. The fear of: who am I if I'm not running? Who am I if I'm not hustling? Who am I if I take off the armor and I'm just—what? A forty-year-old man with no education, no skills, no real plan for how to live differently?

I didn't know.

I'd spent so long being what the world said I was that I didn't know what was underneath anymore. The armor had been on so long that I'd forgotten there was a person under there somewhere.

Three years minimum. Probably more. Years of concrete and steel and fluorescent lights and the sound of doors locking behind you.

I sat on the bunk and I let the weight of it settle.

And for the first time in longer than I could remember, I didn't have a comeback. I didn't have a plan. I didn't have a way to make it feel smaller.

Just the cell. Just the time. Just the understanding that I'd finally run out of road.
