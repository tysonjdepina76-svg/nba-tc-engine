# CHAPTER 10A — THE RUNNING

*May 2009 – August 2011. The chase.*

---

May 8, 2009. MDC Brooklyn.

I walked out of that building and into the sun and I didn't know what to do with it. Freedom. The word I'd dreamed about in cells, in count, in the long nights when the years stretched out ahead of me like a sentence I couldn't finish. I stood there on the sidewalk with my belongings in a bag and the world in front of me and I didn't know what to do with any of it.

But I had a date. February 2010. The continuance. The court date that would clear the warrant, that would let me move forward, that would let me start actually living instead of just surviving the legal system.

I went home to New Bedford. To my mother's house. To the West End that had raised me and watched me and let me come back every time the system spit me out.

I stayed clean. That's what you do when you're on paper—you stay clean, you pass your tests, you show up to your appointments, you give them no reason to violate you. I did everything right. For months, I did everything right.

February 2010.

The warrant came anyway.

---

The barbershop on Rivet Street was where I heard about it. I was getting my hair cut—the same barber who'd been cutting it for years, the same chairs, the same conversations about the neighborhood, about who was locked up, who was out, who was dead. Rabbit was there. Friend of a friend. We'd talked before.

He had a problem with me. Something I said, something I did, something that escalated the way things escalate when men with pride and history are trying to figure out who's hard and who's not. I didn't think much of it. The barbershop was neutral ground. We were both getting our hair cut.

But then Rabbit pulled out a gun.

I saw it before he could aim. I don't remember the exact sequence—the shock, the adrenaline, the training that kicked in before conscious thought—but I know I was moving toward him before the first shot. The barber was screaming. People were diving behind chairs. And Rabbit was trying to get the gun up and I was trying to get to him before he could fire.

I got to him first.

The fight spilled out onto Rivet Street. The gun went off—I don't know if it was him or me or someone else—but the sound was massive in that small space. And then we were outside, grappling, and the gun was on the ground somewhere and Rabbit was bigger than me but I was faster, I was more desperate, and when you survive as many close calls as I had, you learn to fight like your life depends on it.

Because it does.

The gun was mine when the police arrived. That's how it works. The person holding it, the person closest to it—that's who it belongs to. I got charged with the gun. Rabbit got away.

I went back inside.

---

Bristol County. Not Plymouth this time. The warrant from February—the old charge that had been waiting for me since 2010—now combined with a new gun charge from the barbershop. I was twenty-nine years old and I was facing more time. More years. More walls.

The barbershop had been a trap. I didn't see it then, but I understand it now. Rabbit had been waiting for me. Rabbit had known where I'd be, when I'd be there, how to make sure the gun ended up in my hand instead of his. The setup. The play. The thing that happens when you're marked and you don't know it.

Bristol County. February through August 2011. Different facility, same system. The hearings, the continuances, the lawyers, the waiting. Watching other men get released while your case keeps getting pushed. The weight of time that doesn't move any faster just because you're counting it.

August 2011.

Release.

---

The three years between May 2009 and August 2011—I wasn't free. Not really. I was in transition. In and out. In again. The system working me through its machine, breaking me down piece by piece, teaching me that the outside wasn't really the outside as long as I was still connected to it.

But August 2011 changed that.

When I walked out of Bristol County in August 2011, I was done. Done with the system. Done with the courts. Done with being processed, warehoused, pushed through.

The case was resolved. The time was served. I was free.

Free to figure out what that meant.

---

Five years.

From August 2011 to November 2016. Five years of walking around, breathing air, making choices. Five years that felt like borrowed time—every day a gift I hadn't earned, every morning a chance I didn't deserve.

Five years to become someone different.

Five years that I mostly wasted.

But that's the next chapter.
