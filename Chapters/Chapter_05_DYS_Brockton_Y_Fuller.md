# CHAPTER 5 — DYS, BROCKTON Y, FULLER

*The school of how to be a prisoner.*

---

Brockton Y wasn't like the movies. There were no dramatic shankings in the shower. No blood on the walls. No guards looking the other way while things happened that weren't supposed to happen. It was worse than the movies because it was slower. It was bureaucratic. It was a system designed to process you, warehouse you, and send you back out slightly more broken than when you came in.

I was fourteen years old and they put me in a facility with guys who were seventeen, eighteen, nineteen. Old enough to have been through the system already. Old enough to know things I didn't. Old enough to teach me.

That's what DYS was, in the end. A finishing school. Not for academics—I might as well have been illiterate for all the schoolwork I did in those years. But I graduated with honors in the things that mattered inside: the code, the hierarchy, the survival skills that kept you alive when the environment wanted you dead.

The first lesson came fast.

Don't make eye contact with the wrong person. Don't ask questions about anyone's crime. Don't owe anyone anything—no cigarettes, no food, no favors—because debt in a locked facility is a leash. The moment you owe someone something, they own you. And ownership in DYS isn't metaphorical. It's physical, psychological, absolute.

I learned fast. We all did. You didn't survive Brockton Y by being slow.

---

The thirty-day psych eval at Fuller Memorial Hospital was something else entirely.

Thirty days. Thirty days of doctors and therapists and caseworkers watching me, testing me, asking me questions I didn't understand the answers to. What do you think about when you can't sleep? Do you have dreams that scare you? Do you ever feel like hurting yourself or someone else?

I told them what I thought they wanted to hear. That's another skill you learn fast in the system: performance. Give them the answers that get you out fastest, the responses that don't raise flags, the behavior that says I'm fine, I'm compliant, I'm not a problem.

What I didn't tell them—what I couldn't tell them because I didn't have the language for it yet—was that I was grieving. That I had been grieving since I was four years old and nobody helped me stop. That every loss I'd experienced—Tanisha, my grandfather, my great-grandmother—had stacked on top of each other until I was carrying a weight I didn't know how to put down.

I didn't know what to call it then. I called it rage. I called it hardness. I called it the thing that made me different from other kids, the thing that made me harder, stronger, better equipped to survive.

I called it my edge.

It wasn't until I was forty-eight years old, sitting in a federal prison cell, that I finally named it correctly.

Grief. Unprocessed, unhealed, weaponized grief. The kind that turns into armor because armor is easier to carry than pain.

---

The foster home was worse than Brockton Y.

They moved me there after the eval. A house in Bridgewater—ironic, looking back, because Bridgewater State Prison was right down the road. The house sat across from the SECC parking lot, and every day I could see the prison from my window. The fences. The guard towers. The lights.

I didn't know I was looking at my future. I didn't know I'd spend eight years of my life inside facilities like that, only bigger, badder, permanent. But something in me registered it. Some part of my brain that tracked threats, that mapped danger, that learned from the environment what the environment was trying to teach me—I saw that prison and I understood: that's where they send people who don't make it out.

The foster family was... functional. They fed me. Gave me a bed. Made sure I went to whatever appointments DYS required. But they didn't love me. I wasn't their kid. I was a check. A case number. A foster child with a rap sheet who was only there until the system decided what to do with me.

I stayed for a few months. Then DYS released me back to the streets at sixteen, because that's what the system did in the early 1990s—it processed you, held you for a few years, and sent you back out with no support, no plan, no real chance of making it differently than before.

I went home to New Bedford and picked up exactly where I'd left off.

The streets didn't care that I'd been gone. The streets were still there. The corner was still the corner. The people I ran with were older, some of them locked up, some of them dead, but the life—the code, the hustle, the understanding that this was all there was—it was waiting for me like I'd never left.

I was sixteen years old, and the streets said welcome back.

And I answered the call.
