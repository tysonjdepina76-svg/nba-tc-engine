# CHAPTER 1 — MIGUEL AND PAUL G.

*The deaths that taught you to hit first.*

---

I was twelve years old when I learned what the streets do to people you love.

Miguel went first. I don't remember the exact day anymore—the calendar has been lost somewhere between too many birthdays inside and too many nights staring at ceilings that weren't mine. But I remember the sound his mother made. Not a scream. Something worse. A sound that comes from a place where words don't reach. The kind of sound that tells you something has broken that can never be fixed.

Paul G. came after. Close enough to Miguel that the neighborhood felt it in waves—one death landing, another one following before anyone could catch their breath. Paul G. was sharp. Quick with his hands, quicker with his mouth. He could make you laugh when you didn't want to laugh. He could make you forget, for a few minutes, that you were growing up in a place where boys like you didn't get to be boys for long.

Both of them gone. Both of them taken by the same thing that would eventually take so many others—the life we were all caught up in, the current that pulled you under whether you wanted to swim or not.

I remember standing in the West End after each one. The block. The corner. The same corner where we used to joke around, where Paul G. would do his impressions of teachers at normal volume so they could hear him in class, where Miguel would split his food with whoever was hungrier than him. And then the same corner turned into a place where people whispered about what happened, who did it, why.

The neighborhood taught me fast. The streets don't care about you. The streets don't owe you anything. The streets will take your brother, your cousin, your best friend, and keep right on moving like nothing happened. The block keeps spinning. The corner keeps turning. Someone else fills the space. And the people left behind—they learn to hit first or they learn to disappear.

I was twelve. I didn't have words for it then, but I understood: I was next if I didn't move fast enough. I was next if I hesitated. I was next if I let anyone see that I was scared, that I was hurt, that I was a twelve-year-old boy who just lost two people and didn't know where to put the grief.

So I stopped letting anyone see.

I was twelve years old when I learned that love and violence have to live in the same body when you're from the West End. You love hard because these are your people, your block, your blood. And you hit first because the world outside your block will take everything you love if you give it a chance.

Miguel taught me that. Paul G. taught me that. The streets taught me that in the way only the streets can teach—through loss, through blood, through watching people younger than me learn the same lessons I was learning.

There was no counseling. No therapist waiting at the end of the block. No adult sitting me down to explain that grief is not a weakness, that crying doesn't make you soft, that there are ways to carry loss that don't involve becoming a weapon. Just the block. Just the corner. Just the next day and the day after that and the understanding that the world kept moving and I had to move with it or get left behind.

I hit first after that. Every time. Before I even knew what I was doing, my hands were already moving, my fists were already connecting. Not because I wanted to fight. Because I couldn't afford not to. Because the rage underneath—the rage that came from losing Miguel and Paul G. and not having anywhere to put it—was louder than any voice telling me to wait, to think, to step back.

The lesson: the streets take people. And if you don't take them first—if you don't show them you're not the one to mess with—they'll take you too.

I was twelve. And I was already learning how to die slowly while pretending I was still alive.

---

The hardest part was remembering the good parts. The way Miguel laughed—loud, from the belly, the kind of laugh that made everyone around him laugh too. The way Paul G. would look at you when you were down, really down, and say something so stupid that you had no choice but to smile. They were twelve too. We were all twelve. And somewhere in the middle of all that death, we were still just kids who thought we had time.

We didn't.

The streets don't let you be a kid for long.
