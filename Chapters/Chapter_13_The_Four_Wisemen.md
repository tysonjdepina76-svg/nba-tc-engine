# CHAPTER 13 — THE FOUR WISEMEN

*Two dead. Two left. And me — the fifth.*

---

The wisemen were Stanfa's inner circle. His trusted. His people who knew how he operated, what he stood for, how he kept order in a place designed for chaos. When I came into the system, they were already established — men who had been doing this for decades, who had seen everything, who knew exactly who they were and what their roles were.

And then they started dying.

2018. The first one went. Natural causes, I think — or as natural as things get in there. Cancer. The slow take. I watched Stanfa receive the news the same way I'd watched him receive everything: with that stone face, that wall he built so high and so thick that nobody could see over it. But I was watching close now. I was paying attention. And I saw the crack.

2019. The second one. This one hit different. This one I knew better — we had talked, traded stories, shared meals when sharing meals meant something in that place. When he died, I felt it. Not just for him, but for what it meant. Another brick out of Stanfa's wall. Another crack spreading.

2020. The third. By now the defeats were compounding. Each death landing harder than the last. Each one proving something that Stanfa didn't want to believe: that no matter how much power you accumulate, no matter how many walls you build, grief will find you. Loss will finish what the law couldn't.

Two dead. Two left. And me.

---

I wasn't one of the original four. I came later — earned my way into that circle through years of consistent behavior, through proving I could be trusted, through showing that I understood the code. When the third wiseman died, I became the de facto fifth. Not by appointment. By default. By survival.

And I watched what grief was doing to Stanfa.

Here was a man who had built an empire inside the prison system. Who had organized networks that stretched beyond these walls. Who had maintained power and control and order for longer than I'd been alive. And all of it — every wall, every system, every carefully constructed hierarchy — was being dismantled by death. By loss. By the one enemy his armor couldn't stop.

I saw his face during those years. The defeats accumulating. The walls crumbling. The man underneath the title finally being exposed.

And I saw myself in him.

Not the empire. Not the power. Not the control. But the pattern. The way grief compounds. The way loss builds on loss until you're not sure which one is going to finally take you down. The way you build walls to protect yourself from feeling, and then the walls become the prison, and then the walls start to crack, and then—

I had lost Tanisha. Miguel. Paul G. Scotty Wat. My grandfather. My great-grandmother. Shawn G. I had lost years. I had lost myself. I had lost every version of Tyson I might have become if the armor hadn't clicked into place at fourteen. I had been losing my whole life, one piece at a time, and I had never once stopped to count the pieces.

The wisemen were teaching me something nobody in thirty years of incarceration had been able to teach me:

Grief will finish you if you let it.

Loss will take everything if you don't decide to fight back.

But here's the thing — the thing I finally understood, watching Stanfa's defeat and feeling my own grief rising like water in a locked cell:

The lesson is not how to die stronger.

The lesson is how to live longer.

Even when it hurts. Especially when it hurts. The living is the work. The surviving is the victory. Not the dramatic death. Not the noble fall. The quiet choice to get up in the morning when every part of you wants to stay down. The decision to feel the grief instead of running from it. The understanding that being alive — being really, fully, honestly alive — is the scariest and most important thing you'll ever do.

Two wisemen left. Me as the fifth. And Stanfa — defeated, broken, watching his empire crumble one death at a time.

I didn't want to be him.

I didn't want to wait until grief took everything before I decided to fight back.

I didn't want to be a wiseman who died with his armor on.

I wanted — and this is the first time I can remember actually wanting something for myself, something that wasn't about revenge or money or survival — I wanted to live.

Really live.

Not just survive.
