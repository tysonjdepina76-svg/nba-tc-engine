## VI. THE LEGALITY OF THE ISSUES

### A. Due Process — The Foundation of Every Procedure

The 14th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution guarantees that no state shall deprive any person of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. Massachusetts education law and DESE policy extend this principle to school discipline: intervention must precede escalation. Notification is the first step in any process. If notification is broken, every step after it is moot.

The school's own handbook enshrines this principle. Page 44 establishes a mandatory intervention sequence: notify the parent → create an action plan → meet. The school skipped Steps 1, 2, and 3 and proceeded directly to the penalty phase. This is not due process. This is punishment without procedure.

### B. MGL Chapter 119, §21 — Habitual Truancy Definition

Massachusetts law defines a "habitual truant" as a student who willfully fails to attend school for more than 8 school days in a quarter. Angelina's highest quarterly total is 5 unexcused absences (Q1). She has never reached the statutory threshold in any quarter. She is not a habitual truant. The school has no legal basis to process her as one, and no statutory authority to file a CRA petition under MGL Chapter 119, §39E.

### C. MGL Chapter 76, §2 — Parental Liability

State law sets the threshold for parental criminal liability at 7 or more unexcused full school days in a six-month period. Angelina reaches exactly 7 in Semester 1 (5 Q1 + 2 Q2) and 4 in Semester 2. She does not clearly and consistently exceed the statutory threshold. The school has never raised this issue — because it cannot.

### D. DESE Chronic Absenteeism (10% Rule)

The Massachusetts Department of Elementary and Secondary Education defines chronic absenteeism as missing 10% or more of all school days. With 180 school days in the year, the threshold is 18 days. Angelina has 16 total absences — 8.9% of the school year. She is below the state definition of chronic absenteeism. The school cannot classify her as chronically absent under DESE standards.

### E. 603 CMR 23.00 — Student Records

Massachusetts regulations grant parents the right to challenge inaccurate, misleading, or procedurally invalid information in a student's record (603 CMR 23.07, 23.09). The NC/CP designation on Angelina's record is based on attendance data accumulated without the mandatory intervention procedures the school was required to follow. Any record reflecting a penalty imposed without due process is, by definition, an inaccurate record. The parent's right to challenge this record is invoked. The 603 CMR 23.00 timeline is statutory and continues through summer. The principal has until July 1, 2026 to respond. The superintendent has until July 15. A School Committee hearing must occur within 30 days. Summer break does not pause, toll, or extend any of these deadlines.

### F. The School Circumvented Its Own Policy

The handbook establishes a process designed to catch students before they fail. The school applied only the penalty phase of that process — punishment — while suppressing every phase designed to prevent the student from reaching the penalty. This is not an error in execution. It is a structural failure. The system recorded Angelina's absences. It tracked her codes. It printed her reports. It simply refused to act on them — for eight months — and then acted only to impose a consequence.

When a school circumvents its own pre-penalty procedures, the penalty itself is procedurally void. The policy does not say "notify unless inconvenient." It does not say "create an action plan unless the year is almost over." It says what it says. The school ignored it.
