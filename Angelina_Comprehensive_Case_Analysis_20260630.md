## 8. STRONGEST ARGUMENTS AGAINST THE SCHOOL'S LEGAL TEAM

The school's likely "within policy" defense rests on: (a) student had 16 absences, (b) p. 45 NC trigger met at 9 total days, (c) summer school is a legitimate consequence. **This defense fails on five independent grounds:**

**Argument 1 — The penalty is the direct product of the school's own procedural failure (causation / "unclean hands").** The only reason Angelina reached 9 total absences is that the school suppressed the 10/27/25 intervention. The school's silence is the **cause** of the penalty-trigger, not an excuse for it. Equitable estoppel / unclean hands: a school cannot create the condition it then penalizes.

**Argument 2 — The handbook is void for vagueness / internal contradiction as applied (Categories 10–13).** p. 43 says no differentiation; p. 44 keys every intervention to unexcused; p. 45 imposes NC at 9 total but bars credit retrieval at 20 unexcused. The school cannot simultaneously claim the distinction does not exist and rely on it. Ambiguity in school-imposed rules is construed against the school.

**Argument 3 — Statutory thresholds not met (Categories 14–16).** Habitual truancy: NO. Parent liability: NO (exactly at, not above). Chronic absenteeism: NO (8.9% < 10%). The school is using internal handbook triggers to impose consequences that exceed what Massachusetts statutes authorize. Where the legislature has drawn a line, the school cannot redraw it.

**Argument 4 — Procedural due process under § 37H, § 37H½, and 14th Amendment (Categories 17–19; § 4 above).** Even if the school had authority to impose a credit penalty, it had to follow *some* process. It followed none. *Goss v. Lopez* requires notice + opportunity to be heard before any removal of educational benefits. None given. **The 14th Amendment argument is the single strongest hook — it cannot be waived by the school and applies regardless of any handbook interpretation.**

**Argument 5 — The 14-day gap (16 vs. 30) is unexplained and changes every threshold calculation.** If the school's "30 days / 48 periods" figure is wrong, then at 30 days, the DESE chronic-absenteeism threshold (18) IS met — but the PowerSchool system says 16. **The school must explain the 14-day gap before any penalty is valid.** The burden of proof on data accuracy is on the school, not the parent. (Source: PowerSchool Analysis, "The 14-Day Gap.")

## 13. STRONGEST LEGAL ARGUMENTS — RANKED

1. **14th Amendment procedural due process (*Goss v. Lopez*).** A credit designation on a transcript is a property interest. Notice + opportunity to be heard are mandatory. **Neither was provided.** This is the strongest hook because it is constitutional, cannot be waived by the school, and is independent of any handbook interpretation.

2. **The school created the condition it penalized (causation / unclean hands).** The 9-day NC trigger exists only because the school suppressed the 10/27/25 intervention. Equity will not let a party benefit from its own procedural failure.

3. **Statutory thresholds not met.** Habitual truancy (§ 21), parent liability (§ 2), chronic absenteeism (DESE 10%), credit-retrieval bar (p. 45 20-unexcused) — **none met.** The school is using its internal handbook to impose a consequence the legislature did not authorize.

4. **The 16 vs. 30 gap.** PowerSchool says 16; school claims 30. **Until the school explains the 14-day gap, no penalty is valid.** The burden of proof is on the school. At 16 days, the chronic-absenteeism argument is decisive (8.9% < 10%).

5. **Handbook self-contradictions render it unenforceable as applied (p. 43 vs. 44–45).** Ambiguity is construed against the drafter. The school cannot rely on a distinction it claims does not exist.

6. **§ 1B and p. 44 mandatory intervention never executed.** "Shall" is mandatory. Zero notifications, zero action plans, zero meetings. Procedural void.

7. **MGL c. 71 § 28 — summer school is not compulsory.** Cited in 6/23 letter; should be in the appeal.

8. **603 CMR 23.07 + FERPA § 99.20 amendment request as a procedural wedge.** The school must respond by 7/1/26. Failure to respond = further violation; grounds for FERPA complaint and DESE escalation.

9. **§ 37H and § 37H½ by analogy.** The recognized procedural floor for student-facing removal in Massachusetts. The credit penalty is a removal from educational privileges; it should trigger the same procedural protections (written notice, hearing, appeal timeline, right to counsel, right to present evidence).

10. **Bullying → attendance causation (if documented).** Q1 absences followed known bullying; Q4 absences dropped to 1. The school cannot ignore the documented context.
